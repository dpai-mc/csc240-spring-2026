Multinomial Coefficients and Stars & Bars

CSC 240 – Discrete Structures  |  Spring 2026  |  Mendocino College

Estimated time: 2.0 hours  |  CSLO 2: Apply combinatorics and set theory to counting problems.

Objective 4 – Compute multinomial coefficients; count arrangements with repetition Objective 5 – Apply stars & bars to distribute objects into labeled bins

Where We've Been — Where We're Going

In Modules 8.1 and 8.2 you mastered the Binomial Theorem and its coefficient identities. Every expansion you computed involved exactly two terms, a and b, and the coefficients were binomial coefficients C(n, k). But many real counting problems involve more than two categories — more than two variables, more than two types of object, more than two bins to fill.

This module develops two powerful tools for those situations. The Multinomial Theorem generalizes the Binomial Theorem to sums of r ≥ 2 terms, and the Stars and Bars method gives a clean combinatorial model for distributing identical objects into distinct labeled bins. Both tools share a common thread: they count arrangements by dividing out redundancy — the same idea behind the division rule you saw in Week 7.

1 · Motivation — Why We Need More Than Two Terms

Consider the polynomial (x + y + z)³. When we multiply out all three factors, we get terms like x²y, xyz, and z³. Each term corresponds to a choice of which variable to "pick" from each of the three factors. But now there are three choices per factor instead of two, so the binomial coefficient C(n, k) is no longer sufficient to count how many times each monomial appears.

🔍 Motivating Question

In the expansion of (x + y + z)³, what is the coefficient of xyz?

To form xyz we must pick x from one factor, y from another, and z from the third. There are 3! = 6 ways to assign the three factors to the three variables — so the coefficient is 6. The Multinomial Theorem will give us this systematically.

From Binomial to Trinomial to Multinomial

The progression from two terms to many is natural. Each step generalizes the previous one:

Expansion Number of terms (r) General coefficient Example coefficient
(a + b)n r = 2 C(n, k) C(5, 2) = 10
(x + y + z)n r = 3 C(n; k1, k2, k3) C(6; 2, 3, 1) = 60
(x1 + x2 + ⋯ + xr)n any r C(n; k1, k2, …, kr) general formula below

The same generalization applies to counting arrangements. When we ask "how many distinct ways can the letters of MISSISSIPPI be arranged?", we are asking how many ways to distribute 11 letter-slots among four letter-types (M, I, S, P) — a multinomial counting problem.

2 · The Multinomial Theorem

Formal Statement

Theorem — The Multinomial Theorem

Let x1, x2, …, xr be any real numbers and let n be a non-negative integer. Then:

(x1 + x2 + ⋯ + xr)n  =  ∑ C(n; k1, k2, …, kr) · x1k1 · x2k2 · ⋯ · xrkr

where the sum is over all r-tuples of non-negative integers (k1, k2, …, kr) with k1 + k2 + ⋯ + kr = n and ki ≥ 0.

The Multinomial Coefficient Formula

The multinomial coefficient is defined as:

C(n; k1, k2, …, kr)  =  n! k1! · k2! · ⋯ · kr!   where k1 + k2 + ⋯ + kr = n, ki ≥ 0.

Anatomy of the General Term

C(n; k1, k2, …, kr) · x1k1 · x2k2 · ⋯ · xrkr
n — total exponent on the whole expression
r — number of distinct variables (terms)
ki — power assigned to variable xi; must sum to n
n! / (k1! · ⋯ · kr!) — the multinomial coefficient; counts distinct arrangements

Why Does the Formula Count Arrangements?

The multinomial coefficient has a direct combinatorial interpretation as the number of ways to arrange n objects when there are r types with repetition counts k1, k2, …, kr:

  • Start with n labeled positions (slots).
  • Choose k1 slots for the first type: C(n, k1) ways.
  • From the remaining n − k1 slots, choose k2 for the second type: C(n − k1, k2) ways.
  • Continue until all slots are assigned.

Multiplying these choices together:

C(n, k1) · C(n−k1, k2) · C(n−k1−k2, k3) · ⋯  =  n!k1! · (n−k1)! · (n−k1)!k2! · (n−k1−k2)! · ⋯  =  n!k1! · k2! · ⋯ · kr!

The intermediate factorials cancel telescopically, leaving the multinomial coefficient formula. This is exactly the division rule from Week 7 applied iteratively: we divide out the redundancy caused by identical objects within each group.

✅ Key Insight — Multinomial Coefficients Count Arrangements
C(n; k1, k2, …, kr) equals the number of distinct ways to arrange n objects where k1 are of type 1, k2 are of type 2, …, and kr are of type r. Identical objects within the same type are indistinguishable, so we divide by ki! for each type.

3 · Connection to Binomial Coefficients — The r = 2 Special Case

The binomial coefficient is not a separate concept from the multinomial coefficient — it is the special case when there are exactly two variable types (r = 2).

Algebraic Reduction

Set r = 2, k1 = k, and k2 = n − k in the multinomial coefficient formula:

C(n; k1, k2)  =  n!k1! · k2!  =  n!k! · (n−k)!  =  C(n, k)

The multinomial theorem at r = 2 becomes:

(x1 + x2)n  =  ∑k=0n C(n, k) · x1k · x2n−k

which is exactly the Binomial Theorem from Module 8.1.

Side-by-Side Visual Comparison

Binomial (r = 2)

Expression: (a + b)n
Coefficient: C(n, k) = n!k! · (n−k)!
Constraint: k + (n−k) = n
Example: (x + y)4
 coefficient of x²y²: C(4, 2) = 6

Multinomial (r ≥ 2)

Expression: (x1 + x2 + ⋯ + xr)n
Coefficient: C(n; k1,…,kr) = n!k1! · ⋯ · kr!
Constraint: k1 + k2 + ⋯ + kr = n
Example: (x + y + z)4
 coefficient of x²yz: C(4; 2,1,1) = 4!/(2!·1!·1!) = 12

📌 Unifying Principle
Every binomial coefficient is a multinomial coefficient. When you see C(n, k), you can always think of it as C(n; k, n−k) — distributing n objects into exactly two groups of sizes k and n−k. The multinomial framework simply removes the restriction that there are only two groups.

4 · Worked Examples

Goal: Identify the multinomial coefficient for the term x²y³z¹ in the expansion of (x + y + z)⁶.

  1. 1 Identify the parameters. We have n = 6, r = 3 variables (x, y, z), and the target exponents k1 = 2, k2 = 3, k3 = 1.
  2. 2 Verify the constraint. k1 + k2 + k3 = 2 + 3 + 1 = 6 = n. ✓
  3. 3 Apply the multinomial coefficient formula.
    C(6; 2, 3, 1) = 6!2! · 3! · 1!
  4. 4 Compute.
    6!2! · 3! · 1! = 7202 · 6 · 1 = 72012 = 60
The coefficient of x²y³z in (x + y + z)⁶ is 60.
⚠️ Common Mistake
Make sure all exponents sum to n before computing. If k1 + k2 + k3 ≠ n, that term does not appear in the expansion and its coefficient is 0.

Goal: List every distinct monomial type and its coefficient in (a + b + c)³. Here n = 3, r = 3.

We need all non-negative integer triples (k1, k2, k3) with k1 + k2 + k3 = 3:

(k1, k2, k3) Monomial Coefficient 3! / (k1! k2! k3!)
(3,0,0) and permutationsa³, b³, c³3!/(3!·0!·0!) = 1 each
(2,1,0) and permutationsa²b, a²c, b²a, b²c, c²a, c²b3!/(2!·1!·0!) = 3 each
(1,1,1)abc3!/(1!·1!·1!) = 6
(a + b + c)³ = a³ + b³ + c³ + 3(a²b + a²c + b²a + b²c + c²a + c²b) + 6abc

Verify the total number of terms (with repetition): 3 · 1 + 6 · 3 + 1 · 6 = 3 + 18 + 6 = 27 = 3³. ✓ (Each of the 3 factors contributes one of 3 choices, giving 3³ terms before combining like terms.)

Problem: How many distinct arrangements are there of the letters in the word MISSISSIPPI?

  1. 1 Count the letters. MISSISSIPPI has 11 letters total. Take inventory by letter:
LetterCount
M1
I4

Total11
  1. 2 Identify the parameters. n = 11, r = 4 distinct letter types, with kM = 1, kI = 4, kS = 4, kP = 2. Check: 1 + 4 + 4 + 2 = 11. ✓
  2. 3 Apply the formula. If all 11 letters were distinct, there would be 11! arrangements. But the 4 identical I's, 4 identical S's, and 2 identical P's create redundancy. Dividing out the redundancy:
    C(11; 1, 4, 4, 2) = 11!1! · 4! · 4! · 2!
  3. 4 Compute step by step.
    11! = 39,916,800   1! = 1,  4! = 24,  4! = 24,  2! = 2
    1! · 4! · 4! · 2! = 1 · 24 · 24 · 2 = 1,152
    39,916,800 / 1,152 = 34,650
There are 34,650 distinct arrangements of the letters in MISSISSIPPI.
🔗 Connection to Week 7 — The Division Rule
This is the division rule from Week 7 applied to a multiset. We start with 11! arrangements of 11 labeled letters, then divide by ki! for each group of identical letters to remove the overcounting. The multinomial coefficient packages this process into a single formula.

Problem: How many distinct arrangements of the letters in BANANA are there?

  1. 1 Letter inventory: B = 1, A = 3, N = 2. Total = 6 letters. ✓
  2. 2 Parameters: n = 6, kB = 1, kA = 3, kN = 2.
  3. 3
    C(6; 1, 3, 2) = 6!1! · 3! · 2! = 7201 · 6 · 2 = 72012 = 60
There are 60 distinct arrangements of the letters in BANANA.

5 · Applications — Stars and Bars

The multinomial coefficient counts arrangements when objects have distinct types. A complementary question is: how many ways can we distribute n identical (indistinguishable) objects into k distinct labeled bins? This is the domain of the Stars and Bars method.

The Physical Model

Represent the n identical objects as stars (●) and use k − 1 bars (|) as dividers between the k bins. Any arrangement of n stars and k − 1 bars in a row describes exactly one distribution.

Distributing 6 identical objects into 3 labeled bins — three example arrangements:

Bin 1: 2  Bin 2: 1  Bin 3: 3
●● ● ●●●
Bin 1: 0  Bin 2: 4  Bin 3: 2
●●●● ●●
Bin 1: 6  Bin 2: 0  Bin 3: 0
●●● ●●●

Each row shows a sequence of 6 stars and 2 bars (= k − 1 = 3 − 1 bars). Stars to the left of the first bar go into Bin 1; stars between the bars go into Bin 2; stars to the right of the last bar go into Bin 3. A bin can receive 0 stars (two bars adjacent, or a bar at the edge).

Counting the Arrangements — The Formula

We have n stars and k − 1 bars to place in a row — a total of n + k − 1 positions. We simply choose which k − 1 of those positions hold bars (or equivalently, which n positions hold stars):

Stars and Bars — Without Restriction (bins may be empty)

The number of ways to distribute n identical objects into k distinct labeled bins (where bins may receive 0 objects) is:

C(n + k − 1, k − 1)  =  C(n + k − 1, n)

Equivalently: the number of non-negative integer solutions to x1 + x2 + ⋯ + xk = n.

Stars and Bars — With Restriction (each bin gets ≥ 1 object)

If every bin must receive at least one object, "pre-fill" each bin with one star, leaving n − k stars to distribute freely:

C(n − 1, k − 1)

Equivalently: the number of positive integer solutions to x1 + x2 + ⋯ + xk = n with xi ≥ 1. (Requires n ≥ k.)

Problem Translation Guide

🗺️ Four Steps to Set Up Any Stars and Bars Problem
  1. Step 1 — Identify the stars: What is being distributed? How many? This is n.
  2. Step 2 — Identify the bins: How many distinct categories or recipients are there? This is k.
  3. Step 3 — Check constraints: Can a bin receive 0 objects (use C(n+k−1, k−1))? Or must each bin receive at least 1 (use C(n−1, k−1))?
  4. Step 4 — Apply the formula and compute.

Constraint Variations

Strategy — Substitution: If each bin must receive at least m ≥ 1 objects, substitute yi = xi − m (so yi ≥ 0). The constraint x1 + ⋯ + xk = n with xi ≥ m becomes y1 + ⋯ + yk = n − mk with yi ≥ 0.

Example: Distribute 12 identical candies among 4 children so each child gets at least 2.

Substitute yi = xi − 2: distribute 12 − 4 · 2 = 4 candies among 4 children with no restriction:

C(4 + 4 − 1, 4 − 1) = C(7, 3) = 35

When a bin can hold at most u objects, use inclusion-exclusion with stars and bars.

Example: Distribute 10 identical tasks among 3 workers so no worker gets more than 5 tasks.

Let Ai = arrangements where worker i gets ≥ 6 tasks. By inclusion-exclusion:

|valid| = C(12, 2) − 3 · C(6, 2) + 3 · C(0, 2) − ⋯

(Substitute xi′ = xi − 6 for the "bad" worker, reducing the total by 6 each time, then apply stars and bars to the reduced problem.) Full inclusion-exclusion with stars and bars is covered in Week 9.

Applied Mini-Cases

Problem: A team lead has 10 identical tasks to assign to 4 developers. Each developer must receive at least 1 task. How many ways can this be done?

  1. 1Stars = 10 tasks; Bins = 4 developers; Constraint: xi ≥ 1.
  2. 2Use the restricted formula: C(n−1, k−1) = C(10−1, 4−1) = C(9, 3).
  3. 3C(9, 3) = 9! / (3! · 6!) = (9 · 8 · 7) / 6 = 84.
There are 84 ways to assign the tasks.

Problem: An OS must allocate 12 identical memory blocks across 3 processes. Any process may receive 0 blocks. How many allocations are possible?

  1. 1Stars = 12 blocks; Bins = 3 processes; Constraint: xi ≥ 0.
  2. 2Use the unrestricted formula: C(n+k−1, k−1) = C(12+3−1, 3−1) = C(14, 2).
  3. 3C(14, 2) = (14 · 13) / 2 = 91.
There are 91 possible memory allocations.
💡 Algorithm Analysis Connection
Stars and bars models resource allocation directly. In operating systems and distributed computing, counting valid allocations tells us the size of the search space an allocation algorithm must explore — a key input to complexity analysis (covered further in Module 8.5).

Counting Multisets

A multiset is a collection in which elements may repeat. The number of multisets of size n chosen from a set of k distinct element types is identical to the stars and bars count without restriction:

C(n + k − 1, k − 1)

Example: How many ways can you choose 5 pieces of fruit from a store that stocks apples, bananas, and oranges (unlimited supply of each)? Here n = 5, k = 3: C(5+3−1, 3−1) = C(7, 2) = 21 multisets.

✅ Stars and Bars = Combinations with Repetition
The stars and bars formula C(n+k−1, k−1) is exactly the "combinations with repetition" formula from Module 7.5. Stars and bars simply provides the combinatorial model that explains why that formula is correct.
