🎯 Week 8 Learning Goals

By the end of this week you should be able to do the following. Select each goal to see what it means in practice.

01 State and apply the Binomial Theorem
You can write out the full expansion of $$(a + b)^n$$ using binomial coefficients $$\binom{n}{k}$$, identify any specific term in an expansion without expanding everything, and verify the theorem using small cases. You understand why the coefficients in the expansion are exactly the entries of Pascal's triangle.
02 Use binomial coefficient identities
You can apply key identities — including Pascal's Identity $$\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k-1} + \binom{n-1}{k}$$, the Symmetry Identity $$\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{n-k}$$, and the Vandermonde Identity — to simplify expressions and prove combinatorial equalities. You can also verify these identities with combinatorial (counting) arguments.
03 Compute multinomial coefficients
You can extend the binomial theorem to three or more terms using the Multinomial Theorem. Given an expression $$(x_1 + x_2 + \cdots + x_m)^n$$, you can find the coefficient of any specific term using the multinomial coefficient $$\binom{n}{k_1, k_2, \ldots, k_m} = \frac{n!}{k_1!\, k_2!\, \cdots\, k_m!}$$.
04 Solve distribution problems with Stars and Bars
You can model and solve problems that ask "how many ways can we distribute $$n$$ identical objects into $$k$$ distinct bins?" using the Stars and Bars formula $$\binom{n + k - 1}{k - 1}$$. You can also handle constrained versions (e.g., each bin must receive at least one object) by applying a change of variables before using the formula.
05 Explain what generating functions are and why they matter
You can write the ordinary generating function for a simple sequence, explain how the coefficients of the power series encode counting information, and recognize generating functions as a bridge between combinatorics and algebra. This is an introductory exposure — you will build on this concept in later topics such as recurrence relations.
06 Connect counting to algorithm analysis
You can use combinatorial arguments to count the number of inputs, subsets, or paths that an algorithm might process, and relate those counts to Big-O complexity. For example, you can explain why iterating over all subsets of an $$n$$-element set is $$O(2^n)$$ using the Binomial Theorem, and why the number of ways to choose $$k$$ items from $$n$$ appears naturally in divide-and-conquer analysis.
07 Implement a binomial coefficient calculator (PE-07)
You can write a program in Java or C++ that correctly computes $$\binom{n}{k}$$ using at least two methods: the recursive definition (Pascal's Identity) and the direct factorial formula. Your implementation handles edge cases (e.g., $$k = 0$$, $$k = n$$, large values of $$n$$) and includes appropriate test cases demonstrating correctness.

💡 Key Concepts at a Glance

After completing this week, you should be able to explain each of the following in your own words.

Binomial Theorem
The expansion $$(a+b)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k$$ and how to use it to find any term in a polynomial expansion without multiplying everything out.
Binomial Coefficient Identities
Pascal's Identity, the Symmetry Identity, and the Vandermonde Identity — why they are true and how to use them to simplify counting arguments.
Multinomial Coefficients
Generalizing $$\binom{n}{k}$$ to distributions among more than two groups: $$\frac{n!}{k_1!\, k_2!\, \cdots\, k_m!}$$ and its role in expanding multi-term expressions.
Stars and Bars
A visual, intuitive technique for counting the number of ways to distribute $$n$$ identical items into $$k$$ distinct categories, with or without minimum-quantity constraints.
Generating Functions (Intro)
Encoding a sequence of counts as coefficients of a power series. A preview of a tool you will use more deeply when studying recurrence relations and algorithm analysis later in the course.
Algorithm Analysis Connections
How combinatorial counts — subsets, arrangements, distributions — translate directly into Big-O complexity bounds, and why understanding counting makes you a better algorithm designer.

🔭 Looking Ahead: Week 9 — Discrete Probability

After the Midterm Exam, Week 9 introduces Discrete Probability: probability spaces, events, conditional probability, independence, Bayes' theorem, random variables, and expected value.

Here is the key insight: counting is the engine that drives probability. To compute the probability of an event, you typically need to count how many outcomes are in the event and how many total outcomes exist in the sample space. Every technique you have learned in Weeks 7 and 8 — combinations, permutations, inclusion-exclusion, stars and bars — will appear again inside probability calculations. The stronger your counting skills are coming out of Week 8, the smoother your entry into probability will be.

Week 9 also concludes with Quiz 3, which covers Weeks 7–9 (Counting, Probability, and an introduction to Recursion). Finishing Week 8 well sets you up for success on that quiz too.
