üìö Core Concepts

What is Logical Equivalence?

Definition: Two logical expressions are logically equivalent if they have the same truth value for all possible combinations of their variables.

Notation: We write $$ P \equiv Q $$ to mean "P is logically equivalent to Q"

Example: The expressions $$ P \to Q $$ and $$ \neg P \lor Q $$ are logically equivalent.

P ‚Üí Q ‚â° ¬¨P ‚à® Q

You can verify this using a truth table (from Week 3), or you can use equivalence laws to transform one into the other!

Why Logical Equivalences Matter

Logical equivalences allow us to:

  • Simplify expressions: Make complex logic easier to understand
  • Optimize code: Reduce the number of operations needed
  • Prove correctness: Show that two different implementations do the same thing
  • Transform problems: Convert a problem into an easier equivalent form

üíª Programming Connection

Consider these two code snippets:

// Version 1: Complex if (!(x > 5 && y < 10)) { doSomething(); } // Version 2: Simplified using De Morgan's Law if (x <= 5 || y >= 10) { doSomething(); }

These are logically equivalent! Version 2 is easier to read and understand.

Types of Logical Statements

Tautology: Always True ‚ñº

Definition: A tautology is a statement that is true for all possible truth values of its variables.

Examples:

  • $$ P \lor \neg P $$ ‚Äî "Either P is true or P is false" (always true)
  • $$ (P \to Q) \lor (Q \to P) $$ ‚Äî Always true
  • $$ P \lor \top $$ ‚Äî Always true (where $$ \top $$ represents "true")

Why it matters: Tautologies represent logical truths that are always valid, regardless of the specific situation.

Contradiction: Always False ‚ñº

Definition: A contradiction is a statement that is false for all possible truth values of its variables.

Examples:

  • $$ P \land \neg P $$ ‚Äî "P is both true and false" (impossible)
  • $$ (P \lor Q) \land \neg P \land \neg Q $$ ‚Äî Always false
  • $$ P \land \bot $$ ‚Äî Always false (where $$ \bot $$ represents "false")

Why it matters: Contradictions represent impossible situations. Identifying them helps us find logical errors.

Contingency: Sometimes True, Sometimes False ‚ñº

Definition: A contingency is a statement that is true for some truth value assignments and false for others.

Examples:

  • $$ P \land Q $$ ‚Äî True when both P and Q are true, false otherwise
  • $$ P \to Q $$ ‚Äî False only when P is true and Q is false
  • Most real-world logical statements are contingencies

Why it matters: Contingencies represent normal logical conditions that depend on the specific values of variables.

‚öñÔ∏è Logical Equivalence Laws

These laws are your toolkit for simplifying logical expressions. Memorize them and practice applying them!

Identity Laws

P ‚àß ‚ä§ ‚â° P P ‚à® ‚ä• ‚â° P

Explanation: ANDing with "true" or ORing with "false" doesn't change the expression.

Programming analogy: if (condition && true) is the same as if (condition)

Domination Laws

P ‚à® ‚ä§ ‚â° ‚ä§ P ‚àß ‚ä• ‚â° ‚ä•

Explanation: ORing with "true" always gives true; ANDing with "false" always gives false.

Programming analogy: if (condition || true) is always true

Idempotent Laws

P ‚à® P ‚â° P P ‚àß P ‚â° P

Explanation: ORing or ANDing something with itself doesn't change it.

Example: "It's raining OR it's raining" is the same as "It's raining"

Double Negation Law

¬¨(¬¨P) ‚â° P

Explanation: Two negations cancel each other out.

Programming analogy: !(!condition) is the same as condition

Commutative Laws

P ‚à® Q ‚â° Q ‚à® P P ‚àß Q ‚â° Q ‚àß P

Explanation: The order of OR and AND operations doesn't matter.

Example: "It's sunny AND warm" = "It's warm AND sunny"

Associative Laws

(P ‚à® Q) ‚à® R ‚â° P ‚à® (Q ‚à® R) (P ‚àß Q) ‚àß R ‚â° P ‚àß (Q ‚àß R)

Explanation: Grouping doesn't matter for OR and AND operations.

Note: This means we can write $$ P \lor Q \lor R $$ without parentheses!

Distributive Laws

P ‚àß (Q ‚à® R) ‚â° (P ‚àß Q) ‚à® (P ‚àß R) P ‚à® (Q ‚àß R) ‚â° (P ‚à® Q) ‚àß (P ‚à® R)

Explanation: Similar to distributing multiplication over addition in algebra.

Key difference: The second law (OR distributes over AND) doesn't work in regular algebra!

De Morgan's Laws

¬¨(P ‚àß Q) ‚â° ¬¨P ‚à® ¬¨Q ¬¨(P ‚à® Q) ‚â° ¬¨P ‚àß ¬¨Q

Explanation: To negate an AND/OR, negate each part and flip the operator.

Memory trick: "Break the line, change the sign"

Programming example:

// Original: !(x > 5 && y < 10) // De Morgan: (x <= 5 || y >= 10)

Absorption Laws

P ‚à® (P ‚àß Q) ‚â° P P ‚àß (P ‚à® Q) ‚â° P

Explanation: If P is true, the rest doesn't matter.

Example: "It's raining OR (it's raining AND cold)" = "It's raining"

Negation Laws

P ‚à® ¬¨P ‚â° ‚ä§ P ‚àß ¬¨P ‚â° ‚ä•

Explanation: Something is either true or false (law of excluded middle).

Note: $$ P \lor \neg P $$ is a tautology; $$ P \land \neg P $$ is a contradiction.

Conditional Equivalences

P ‚Üí Q ‚â° ¬¨P ‚à® Q P ‚Üí Q ‚â° ¬¨Q ‚Üí ¬¨P (contrapositive) P ‚Üî Q ‚â° (P ‚Üí Q) ‚àß (Q ‚Üí P)

Explanation: Implications can be rewritten using OR, and biconditionals are two-way implications.

üí° How to Use These Laws

  1. Identify the pattern: Look for expressions that match the left side of a law
  2. Apply the transformation: Replace with the right side of the law
  3. Simplify step-by-step: Apply one law at a time
  4. Document each step: Write which law you used

üìù Quick Reference Table

Law Name Formula Use Case
Identity $$ P \land \top \equiv P $$ Remove unnecessary "true" conditions
Domination $$ P \lor \top \equiv \top $$ Identify always-true conditions
Idempotent $$ P \lor P \equiv P $$ Remove duplicate conditions
Double Negation $$ \neg(\neg P) \equiv P $$ Simplify nested negations
De Morgan's $$ \neg(P \land Q) \equiv \neg P \lor \neg Q $$ Distribute negations
Distributive $$ P \land (Q \lor R) \equiv (P \land Q) \lor (P \land R) $$ Expand or factor expressions
Absorption $$ P \lor (P \land Q) \equiv P $$ Simplify redundant terms
Conditional $$ P \to Q \equiv \neg P \lor Q $$ Eliminate implications

üí° Worked Examples

Let's see these laws in action! Each example shows step-by-step simplification.

Example 1: Simplify $$ \neg(P \land Q) \lor P $$ ‚ñº

Original Expression:

¬¨(P ‚àß Q) ‚à® P

Step-by-Step Simplification:

Step Expression Law Used
1 $$ \neg(P \land Q) \lor P $$ Original
2 $$ (\neg P \lor \neg Q) \lor P $$ De Morgan's Law
3 $$ \neg P \lor \neg Q \lor P $$ Associative Law
4 $$ \neg P \lor P \lor \neg Q $$ Commutative Law
5 $$ \top \lor \neg Q $$ Negation Law: $$ \neg P \lor P \equiv \top $$
6 $$ \top $$ Domination Law

Result: The expression is a tautology (always true)!

Interpretation: Either P is false (making the first part true), or P is true (making the second part true). So the whole expression is always true.

Example 2: Simplify $$ (P \lor Q) \land (P \lor \neg Q) $$ ‚ñº

Original Expression:

(P ‚à® Q) ‚àß (P ‚à® ¬¨Q)

Step-by-Step Simplification:

Step Expression Law Used
1 $$ (P \lor Q) \land (P \lor \neg Q) $$ Original
2 $$ P \lor (Q \land \neg Q) $$ Distributive Law (backwards)
3 $$ P \lor \bot $$ Negation Law: $$ Q \land \neg Q \equiv \bot $$
4 $$ P $$ Identity Law

Result: The expression simplifies to just $$ P $$!

Interpretation: If P is true, both parts are true. If P is false, one part has Q true and the other has Q false, making the AND false. So the result is just P.

Example 3: Simplify $$ \neg(P \to Q) $$ ‚ñº

Original Expression:

¬¨(P ‚Üí Q)

Step-by-Step Simplification:

Step Expression Law Used
1 $$ \neg(P \to Q) $$ Original
2 $$ \neg(\neg P \lor Q) $$ Conditional Equivalence: $$ P \to Q \equiv \neg P \lor Q $$
3 $$ \neg(\neg P) \land \neg Q $$ De Morgan's Law
4 $$ P \land \neg Q $$ Double Negation

Result: $$ \neg(P \to Q) \equiv P \land \neg Q $$

Interpretation: The negation of "if P then Q" is "P is true AND Q is false". This makes sense: an implication is only false when the hypothesis is true but the conclusion is false!

üíª Programming Application

This is useful for understanding when code conditions fail:

Example 4: Simplify $$ (P \land Q) \lor (P \land \neg Q) $$ ‚ñº

Original Expression:

(P ‚àß Q) ‚à® (P ‚àß ¬¨Q)

Step-by-Step Simplification:

Step Expression Law Used
1 $$ (P \land Q) \lor (P \land \neg Q) $$ Original
2 $$ P \land (Q \lor \neg Q) $$ Distributive Law (factoring out P)
3 $$ P \land \top $$ Negation Law: $$ Q \lor \neg Q \equiv \top $$
4 $$ P $$ Identity Law

Result: The expression simplifies to just $$ P $$!

Interpretation: Whether Q is true or false doesn't matter if P is true. This is like saying "Either (it's raining AND sunny) OR (it's raining AND not sunny)" = "It's raining".

Example 5: Simplify $$ \neg((P \lor Q) \land (\neg P \lor \neg Q)) $$ ‚ñº

Original Expression:

¬¨((P ‚à® Q) ‚àß (¬¨P ‚à® ¬¨Q))

Step-by-Step Simplification:

Step Expression Law Used
1 $$ \neg((P \lor Q) \land (\neg P \lor \neg Q)) $$ Original
2 $$ \neg(P \lor Q) \lor \neg(\neg P \lor \neg Q) $$ De Morgan's Law
3 $$ (\neg P \land \neg Q) \lor \neg(\neg P \lor \neg Q) $$ De Morgan's Law on first part
4 $$ (\neg P \land \neg Q) \lor (\neg(\neg P) \land \neg(\neg Q)) $$ De Morgan's Law on second part
5 $$ (\neg P \land \neg Q) \lor (P \land Q) $$ Double Negation (twice)

Result: $$ (\neg P \land \neg Q) \lor (P \land Q) $$

Interpretation: This expression is true when either both P and Q are false, OR both are true. This is actually the biconditional $$ P \leftrightarrow Q $$!

Bonus: We could simplify further to $$ P \leftrightarrow Q $$

üíª Programming Examples

Example 1: Simplifying Conditional Logic

Problem:

Simplify this complex conditional:

if (!(x > 10 && y < 5) && (x > 10 || y >= 5)) { // do something }

Solution:

Let $$ P $$ = "x > 10" and $$ Q $$ = "y < 5"

The condition is: $$ \neg(P \land Q) \land (P \lor \neg Q) $$

Step Expression Law Used
1 $$ \neg(P \land Q) \land (P \lor \neg Q) $$ Original
2 $$ (\neg P \lor \neg Q) \land (P \lor \neg Q) $$ De Morgan's Law
3 $$ (\neg P \land P) \lor (\neg P \land \neg Q) \lor (\neg Q \land P) \lor (\neg Q \land \neg Q) $$ Distributive Law
4 $$ \bot \lor (\neg P \land \neg Q) \lor (P \land \neg Q) \lor \neg Q $$ Negation Law, Idempotent
5 $$ (\neg P \land \neg Q) \lor (P \land \neg Q) \lor \neg Q $$ Identity Law
6 $$ \neg Q $$ Absorption Law (applied twice)

Simplified Code:

if (y >= 5) { // do something }

Result: The complex condition simplifies to just checking if y >= 5!

Example 2: Optimizing Boolean Flags

Problem:

boolean result = (isValid && isActive) || (isValid && !isActive);

Solution:

Let $$ P $$ = "isValid" and $$ Q $$ = "isActive"

Expression: $$ (P \land Q) \lor (P \land \neg Q) $$

From Example 4 above, we know this simplifies to just $$ P $$!

Simplified Code:

boolean result = isValid;

Result: Whether isActive is true or false doesn't matter!
