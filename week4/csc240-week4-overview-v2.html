üìö Week 4 Topics in Detail

Topic 1: Logical Equivalences

Key Concepts:

  • Logical Equivalence: Two propositions are logically equivalent if they have the same truth value in all cases
  • Equivalence Laws: Identity, Domination, Idempotent, Double Negation, Commutative, Associative, Distributive
  • De Morgan's Laws: Rules for negating conjunctions and disjunctions
  • Absorption Laws: Simplification techniques for complex expressions
  • Conditional Equivalences: $$ P \to Q \equiv \neg P \lor Q $$

üîë Essential Equivalence Laws

Identity Laws: P ‚àß ‚ä§ ‚â° P P ‚à® ‚ä• ‚â° P Domination Laws: P ‚à® ‚ä§ ‚â° ‚ä§ P ‚àß ‚ä• ‚â° ‚ä• Idempotent Laws: P ‚à® P ‚â° P P ‚àß P ‚â° P Double Negation: ¬¨(¬¨P) ‚â° P De Morgan's Laws: ¬¨(P ‚àß Q) ‚â° ¬¨P ‚à® ¬¨Q ¬¨(P ‚à® Q) ‚â° ¬¨P ‚àß ¬¨Q Distributive Laws: P ‚àß (Q ‚à® R) ‚â° (P ‚àß Q) ‚à® (P ‚àß R) P ‚à® (Q ‚àß R) ‚â° (P ‚à® Q) ‚àß (P ‚à® R) Absorption Laws: P ‚à® (P ‚àß Q) ‚â° P P ‚àß (P ‚à® Q) ‚â° P Conditional: P ‚Üí Q ‚â° ¬¨P ‚à® Q P ‚Üí Q ‚â° ¬¨Q ‚Üí ¬¨P (contrapositive)

Topic 2: Normal Forms (CNF and DNF)

Key Concepts:

  • Literal: A variable or its negation (P or ¬¨P)
  • Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF): Conjunction of disjunctions (AND of ORs)
  • Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF): Disjunction of conjunctions (OR of ANDs)
  • Conversion Process: Systematic method to convert any proposition to CNF or DNF
  • Applications: SAT solvers, circuit design, Boolean algebra

üìù CNF vs DNF Examples

Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF):

(P ‚à® Q) ‚àß (¬¨P ‚à® R) ‚àß (Q ‚à® ¬¨R) ‚Üë Each clause is a disjunction (OR) ‚Üë Clauses are connected by conjunction (AND)

Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF):

(P ‚àß Q) ‚à® (¬¨P ‚àß R) ‚à® (Q ‚àß ¬¨R) ‚Üë Each term is a conjunction (AND) ‚Üë Terms are connected by disjunction (OR)

Conversion Steps:

  1. Eliminate implications and biconditionals
  2. Move negations inward using De Morgan's laws
  3. Apply distributive laws to achieve desired form
  4. Simplify using equivalence laws

Topic 3: Predicate Logic

Key Concepts:

  • Predicate: A function that returns true or false (e.g., isEven(x), Student(x))
  • Domain of Discourse: The set of all possible values for variables
  • Propositional Functions: Statements with variables that become propositions when values are assigned
  • Free vs Bound Variables: Variables with and without quantifiers
  • Scope of Quantifiers: The portion of a statement affected by a quantifier

üîç Predicate Logic Examples

Simple Predicates:

isEven(4) ‚Üí true isEven(5) ‚Üí false Student(Alice, CSC240) ‚Üí true Student(Bob, MTH80) ‚Üí true

Predicates with Multiple Arguments:

GreaterThan(x, y) ‚Äî "x is greater than y" Enrolled(student, course) ‚Äî "student is enrolled in course" ParentOf(p, c) ‚Äî "p is a parent of c"

Topic 4: Universal and Existential Quantifiers

Key Concepts:

  • Universal Quantifier ($$ \forall $$): "for all," "for every," "for each"
  • Existential Quantifier ($$ \exists $$): "there exists," "for some," "there is at least one"
  • Uniqueness Quantifier ($$ \exists! $$): "there exists exactly one"
  • Domain Specification: Explicitly stating the set of values being quantified over
  • Vacuous Truth: $$ \forall x \, P(x) $$ is true if the domain is empty

‚ú® Quantifier Examples

English Statement Predicate Logic Notes
All integers are rational $$ \forall x (\text{Integer}(x) \to \text{Rational}(x)) $$ Universal with implication
Some students are programmers $$ \exists x (\text{Student}(x) \land \text{Programmer}(x)) $$ Existential with conjunction
Every course has at least one student $$ \forall c \, \exists s \, \text{Enrolled}(s, c) $$ Nested quantifiers
There is a perfect square $$ \exists x \, \exists y (x = y^2) $$ Multiple existential
No prime number is even except 2 $$ \forall x (\text{Prime}(x) \land \text{Even}(x) \to x = 2) $$ Universal with exception

Topic 5: Nested Quantifiers

Key Concepts:

  • Quantifier Order: The order of quantifiers matters: $$ \forall x \exists y \neq \exists y \forall x $$
  • Reading Nested Quantifiers: Read from left to right, outside to inside
  • Common Patterns: $$ \forall \forall $$ (all-all), $$ \exists \exists $$ (some-some), $$ \forall \exists $$ (all-some), $$ \exists \forall $$ (some-all)
  • Negating Nested Quantifiers: Apply De Morgan's laws repeatedly

‚ö†Ô∏è Order Matters!

Example: Difference in quantifier order

‚àÄx ‚àÉy (x + y = 0) "For every x, there exists a y such that x + y = 0" TRUE: For any number x, we can find y = -x ‚àÉy ‚àÄx (x + y = 0) "There exists a y such that for all x, x + y = 0" FALSE: No single y works for all x

Negation Example:

Original: ‚àÄx ‚àÉy P(x, y) Step 1: ¬¨‚àÄx ‚àÉy P(x, y) Step 2: ‚àÉx ¬¨‚àÉy P(x, y) [Negate ‚àÄ ‚Üí ‚àÉ] Step 3: ‚àÉx ‚àÄy ¬¨P(x, y) [Negate ‚àÉ ‚Üí ‚àÄ]

Topic 6: Negation of Quantified Statements

Key Concepts:

  • De Morgan's Laws for Quantifiers: $$ \neg \forall x \, P(x) \equiv \exists x \, \neg P(x) $$ and $$ \neg \exists x \, P(x) \equiv \forall x \, \neg P(x) $$
  • Negating Implications: $$ \neg(P \to Q) \equiv P \land \neg Q $$
  • Step-by-Step Negation: Work from outside to inside, one quantifier at a time
  • Common Errors: Forgetting to negate the predicate, incorrect quantifier swap

üéØ Negation Practice

Original Statement Negation English
$$ \forall x \, P(x) $$ $$ \exists x \, \neg P(x) $$ "Not all x satisfy P" ‚Üí "Some x doesn't satisfy P"
$$ \exists x \, P(x) $$ $$ \forall x \, \neg P(x) $$ "It's not true that some x satisfies P" ‚Üí "No x satisfies P"
$$ \forall x (P(x) \to Q(x)) $$ $$ \exists x (P(x) \land \neg Q(x)) $$ "Not all P's are Q's" ‚Üí "Some P is not Q"
$$ \exists x (P(x) \land Q(x)) $$ $$ \forall x (\neg P(x) \lor \neg Q(x)) $$ "No x is both P and Q"
