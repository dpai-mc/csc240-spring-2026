üìö What You'll Learn

Normal forms are standardized ways of writing logical expressions. Just as you can write numbers in different forms (decimal, fraction, scientific notation), you can write logical expressions in different standard forms. This module focuses on two important normal forms:

  • Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF): "Product of Sums" or "AND of ORs"
  • Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF): "Sum of Products" or "OR of ANDs"

üîó Why Normal Forms Matter

Real-World Applications:

  • SAT Solvers: CNF is the standard input format for Boolean satisfiability solvers used in circuit design, software verification, and AI
  • Digital Circuit Design: Both forms represent circuits with AND/OR gates
  • Database Query Optimization: SQL queries are often converted to normal forms for optimization
  • Automated Theorem Proving: Many proof systems work with clausal forms (CNF)
  • Machine Learning: Decision trees and rule-based systems use DNF-like representations

üìö Core Concepts

Building Blocks: Literals, Clauses, and Terms

üìñ Literal

A literal is either a propositional variable or its negation.

Examples of literals:

  • $$ P $$ (a variable)
  • $$ \neg P $$ (negation of a variable)
  • $$ Q, \neg Q, R, \neg R $$ (more examples)

NOT literals:

  • $$ P \land Q $$ (this is a conjunction, not a literal)
  • $$ \neg \neg P $$ (double negation, not simplified)
  • $$ P \lor Q $$ (this is a disjunction, not a literal)

üìñ Clause (for CNF)

A clause is a disjunction (OR) of literals.

Examples:

  • $$ P \lor Q $$ (clause with 2 literals)
  • $$ P \lor \neg Q \lor R $$ (clause with 3 literals)
  • $$ \neg P \lor \neg Q \lor \neg R \lor S $$ (clause with 4 literals)
  • $$ P $$ (a single literal is also a clause)

üìñ Term (for DNF)

A term is a conjunction (AND) of literals.

Examples:

  • $$ P \land Q $$ (term with 2 literals)
  • $$ P \land \neg Q \land R $$ (term with 3 literals)
  • $$ \neg P \land \neg Q \land \neg R \land S $$ (term with 4 literals)
  • $$ P $$ (a single literal is also a term)

Why Do We Need Normal Forms?

Normal forms provide several advantages:

  • Standardization: Different expressions with the same meaning can be compared easily
  • Simplification: Makes it easier to identify equivalent expressions
  • Algorithmic Processing: Many algorithms require input in a specific normal form
  • Proof Systems: Automated theorem provers often work with CNF
  • Circuit Design: Direct mapping to hardware gates

üíª Programming Connection

Normal forms appear throughout computer science:

// DNF Example: OR of ANDs if ((x > 0 && y > 0) || (x < 0 && y < 0)) { // Same sign } // CNF Example: AND of ORs if ((x > 0 || y > 0) && (x < 0 || y < 0)) { // Different condition } // Database Query (DNF-like) SELECT * FROM students WHERE (major = 'CS' AND gpa > 3.5) OR (major = 'Math' AND gpa > 3.8);

Quick Identification Practice

Example 1: Is $$ (P \lor Q) \land (R \lor S) $$ in CNF or DNF? ‚ñº

Answer: CNF (Conjunctive Normal Form)

Why?

  • The main operator is AND ($$ \land $$)
  • Each part connected by AND is a clause (disjunction of literals)
  • $$ (P \lor Q) $$ is a clause
  • $$ (R \lor S) $$ is a clause
  • Structure: AND of ORs ‚úì
Example 2: Is $$ (P \land Q) \lor (R \land S) $$ in CNF or DNF? ‚ñº

Answer: DNF (Disjunctive Normal Form)

Why?

  • The main operator is OR ($$ \lor $$)
  • Each part connected by OR is a term (conjunction of literals)
  • $$ (P \land Q) $$ is a term
  • $$ (R \land S) $$ is a term
  • Structure: OR of ANDs ‚úì
Example 3: Is $$ P \land Q \land R $$ in CNF, DNF, or both? ‚ñº

Answer: BOTH CNF and DNF

Why?

  • As CNF: It's a conjunction of clauses where each clause has one literal
  • As DNF: It's a disjunction of one term (that term has three literals)
  • When an expression has only one "level" of operators, it can be both
  • Simple expressions like $$ P $$, $$ P \land Q $$, or $$ P \lor Q $$ are both CNF and DNF
Example 4: Is $$ P \lor (Q \land R) $$ in CNF or DNF? ‚ñº

Answer: NEITHER

Why?

  • For DNF: The main operator is OR, which is good...
  • BUT: One part is $$ P $$ (a literal, OK) and the other is $$ (Q \land R) $$ (a term, OK)
  • Wait, that should be DNF! Let me reconsider...
  • Actually, this IS DNF! It's $$ P \lor (Q \land R) $$, which is a disjunction of terms
  • Term 1: $$ P $$ (single literal term)
  • Term 2: $$ (Q \land R) $$ (conjunction of literals)
  • Correction: This IS in DNF

Not CNF because: To be CNF, we'd need AND of ORs, but here we have OR at the top level with AND nested inside

üî∑ Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF)

CNF Structure

(clause‚ÇÅ) ‚àß (clause‚ÇÇ) ‚àß (clause‚ÇÉ) ‚àß ... Where each clause is: (literal ‚à® literal ‚à® literal ‚à® ...)

What is CNF?

Definition: A proposition is in Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF) if it is a conjunction (AND) of one or more clauses, where each clause is a disjunction (OR) of literals.

Also called: "Product of Sums" (from Boolean algebra)

Pattern: $$ (L_{11} \lor L_{12} \lor \ldots) \land (L_{21} \lor L_{22} \lor \ldots) \land \ldots $$

CNF Examples

Example 1: Simple CNF ‚ñº
(P ‚à® Q) ‚àß (¬¨P ‚à® R)

Analysis:

  • Main operator: AND ($$ \land $$) ‚úì
  • Clause 1: $$ P \lor Q $$ (disjunction of literals) ‚úì
  • Clause 2: $$ \neg P \lor R $$ (disjunction of literals) ‚úì
  • This is in CNF
Example 2: Three-Clause CNF ‚ñº
(P ‚à® Q ‚à® R) ‚àß (¬¨P ‚à® ¬¨Q) ‚àß (Q ‚à® ¬¨R)

Analysis:

  • Main operator: AND connecting three clauses ‚úì
  • Clause 1: $$ P \lor Q \lor R $$ (3 literals) ‚úì
  • Clause 2: $$ \neg P \lor \neg Q $$ (2 literals) ‚úì
  • Clause 3: $$ Q \lor \neg R $$ (2 literals) ‚úì
  • This is in CNF
Example 3: NOT in CNF ‚ñº
(P ‚àß Q) ‚à® R

Why NOT CNF?

  • Main operator: OR ($$ \lor $$) ‚úó
  • For CNF, we need AND at the top level
  • This has AND nested inside an OR
  • This is actually in DNF, not CNF

Converting to CNF: Step-by-Step Algorithm

üîß CNF Conversion Algorithm

  1. Eliminate implications and biconditionals:
    • $$ P \to Q \equiv \neg P \lor Q $$
    • $$ P \leftrightarrow Q \equiv (P \to Q) \land (Q \to P) $$
  2. Move negations inward (De Morgan's Laws):
    • $$ \neg(P \land Q) \equiv \neg P \lor \neg Q $$
    • $$ \neg(P \lor Q) \equiv \neg P \land \neg Q $$
    • $$ \neg \neg P \equiv P $$
  3. Distribute OR over AND:
    • $$ P \lor (Q \land R) \equiv (P \lor Q) \land (P \lor R) $$
    • $$ (P \land Q) \lor R \equiv (P \lor R) \land (Q \lor R) $$
  4. Simplify using equivalence laws (optional but recommended)

Worked Example: Converting to CNF

0Original Expression:

(P ‚Üí Q) ‚àß (Q ‚Üí R)

1Eliminate implications:

Apply $$ P \to Q \equiv \neg P \lor Q $$

(¬¨P ‚à® Q) ‚àß (¬¨Q ‚à® R)

Result: Already in CNF! ‚úì

Final CNF: $$ (\neg P \lor Q) \land (\neg Q \lor R) $$

This is CNF because:

  • Main operator is AND
  • Each clause is a disjunction of literals
  • No further simplification needed

Complex Example: Converting to CNF

0Original Expression:

¬¨((P ‚àß Q) ‚Üí R)

1Eliminate implication:

¬¨(¬¨(P ‚àß Q) ‚à® R)

2Apply De Morgan's Law to outer negation:

$$ \neg(A \lor B) \equiv \neg A \land \neg B $$

¬¨¬¨(P ‚àß Q) ‚àß ¬¨R

3Apply double negation:

(P ‚àß Q) ‚àß ¬¨R

4Simplify (associativity of AND):

P ‚àß Q ‚àß ¬¨R

Final CNF: $$ P \land Q \land \neg R $$

This is CNF because:

  • It's a conjunction of clauses
  • Each "clause" is a single literal (which counts as a clause)
  • Could write as: $$ (P) \land (Q) \land (\neg R) $$ to make it clearer

Converting from Truth Tables to CNF

Method: Use Maxterms

A maxterm is a clause that is false for exactly one row of the truth table.

Algorithm:

  1. Find all rows where the expression is FALSE
  2. For each FALSE row, create a maxterm:
    • If variable is TRUE in that row, use its negation
    • If variable is FALSE in that row, use the variable itself
    • OR all the literals together
  3. AND all the maxterms together

Example: Truth Table to CNF

Suppose we want CNF for an expression that is FALSE only when:

  • Row 1: $$ P = T, Q = T $$ (expression is FALSE)
  • Row 2: $$ P = F, Q = F $$ (expression is FALSE)
P Q Expression Maxterm (if FALSE)
T T F $$ \neg P \lor \neg Q $$
T F T ‚Äî
F T T ‚Äî
F F F $$ P \lor Q $$

CNF: $$ (\neg P \lor \neg Q) \land (P \lor Q) $$

üí° Why This Works

Each maxterm is FALSE for exactly one row. By ANDing them together, the entire expression is TRUE only when ALL maxterms are TRUE, which happens for all rows except the FALSE rows we identified.

üî∂ Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF)

DNF Structure

(term‚ÇÅ) ‚à® (term‚ÇÇ) ‚à® (term‚ÇÉ) ‚à® ... Where each term is: (literal ‚àß literal ‚àß literal ‚àß ...)

What is DNF?

Definition: A proposition is in Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF) if it is a disjunction (OR) of one or more terms, where each term is a conjunction (AND) of literals.

Also called: "Sum of Products" (from Boolean algebra)

Pattern: $$ (L_{11} \land L_{12} \land \ldots) \lor (L_{21} \land L_{22} \land \ldots) \lor \ldots $$

DNF Examples

Example 1: Simple DNF ‚ñº
(P ‚àß Q) ‚à® (¬¨P ‚àß R)

Analysis:

  • Main operator: OR ($$ \lor $$) ‚úì
  • Term 1: $$ P \land Q $$ (conjunction of literals) ‚úì
  • Term 2: $$ \neg P \land R $$ (conjunction of literals) ‚úì
  • This is in DNF
Example 2: Three-Term DNF ‚ñº
(P ‚àß Q ‚àß R) ‚à® (¬¨P ‚àß ¬¨Q) ‚à® (Q ‚àß ¬¨R)

Analysis:

  • Main operator: OR connecting three terms ‚úì
  • Term 1: $$ P \land Q \land R $$ (3 literals) ‚úì
  • Term 2: $$ \neg P \land \neg Q $$ (2 literals) ‚úì
  • Term 3: $$ Q \land \neg R $$ (2 literals) ‚úì
  • This is in DNF
Example 3: NOT in DNF ‚ñº
(P ‚à® Q) ‚àß R

Why NOT DNF?

  • Main operator: AND ($$ \land $$) ‚úó
  • For DNF, we need OR at the top level
  • This has OR nested inside an AND
  • This is actually in CNF, not DNF

Converting to DNF: Step-by-Step Algorithm

üîß DNF Conversion Algorithm

  1. Eliminate implications and biconditionals:
    • $$ P \to Q \equiv \neg P \lor Q $$
    • $$ P \leftrightarrow Q \equiv (P \to Q) \land (Q \to P) $$
  2. Move negations inward (De Morgan's Laws):
    • $$ \neg(P \land Q) \equiv \neg P \lor \neg Q $$
    • $$ \neg(P \lor Q) \equiv \neg P \land \neg Q $$
    • $$ \neg \neg P \equiv P $$
  3. Distribute AND over OR:
    • $$ P \land (Q \lor R) \equiv (P \land Q) \lor (P \land R) $$
    • $$ (P \lor Q) \land R \equiv (P \land R) \lor (Q \land R) $$
  4. Simplify using equivalence laws (optional but recommended)

Worked Example: Converting to DNF

0Original Expression:

(P ‚à® Q) ‚àß R

1Distribute AND over OR:

Apply $$ (P \lor Q) \land R \equiv (P \land R) \lor (Q \land R) $$

(P ‚àß R) ‚à® (Q ‚àß R)

Result: Now in DNF! ‚úì

Final DNF: $$ (P \land R) \lor (Q \land R) $$

This is DNF because:

  • Main operator is OR
  • Each term is a conjunction of literals
  • Structure: OR of ANDs ‚úì

Complex Example: Converting to DNF

0Original Expression:

(P ‚Üí Q) ‚àß (Q ‚Üí R)

1Eliminate implications:

(¬¨P ‚à® Q) ‚àß (¬¨Q ‚à® R)

2Distribute AND over OR:

Use $$ (A \lor B) \land (C \lor D) \equiv (A \land C) \lor (A \land D) \lor (B \land C) \lor (B \land D) $$

(¬¨P ‚àß ¬¨Q) ‚à® (¬¨P ‚àß R) ‚à® (Q ‚àß ¬¨Q) ‚à® (Q ‚àß R)

3Simplify (eliminate contradiction):

$$ Q \land \neg Q \equiv \bot $$ (contradiction, always false)

(¬¨P ‚àß ¬¨Q) ‚à® (¬¨P ‚àß R) ‚à® (Q ‚àß R)

Final DNF: $$ (\neg P \land \neg Q) \lor (\neg P \land R) \lor (Q \land R) $$

This is DNF because:

  • Main operator is OR
  • Each term is a conjunction of literals
  • All contradictions removed

Converting from Truth Tables to DNF

Method: Use Minterms

A minterm is a term that is true for exactly one row of the truth table.

Algorithm:

  1. Find all rows where the expression is TRUE
  2. For each TRUE row, create a minterm:
    • If variable is TRUE in that row, use the variable itself
    • If variable is FALSE in that row, use its negation
    • AND all the literals together
  3. OR all the minterms together

Example: Truth Table to DNF

Suppose we want DNF for an expression that is TRUE only when:

  • Row 1: $$ P = T, Q = F $$ (expression is TRUE)
  • Row 2: $$ P = F, Q = T $$ (expression is TRUE)
P Q Expression Minterm (if TRUE)
T T F ‚Äî
T F T $$ P \land \neg Q $$
F T T $$ \neg P \land Q $$
F F F ‚Äî

DNF: $$ (P \land \neg Q) \lor (\neg P \land Q) $$

üí° Why This Works

Each minterm is TRUE for exactly one row. By ORing them together, the entire expression is TRUE when ANY minterm is TRUE, which happens for exactly the TRUE rows we identified.

Note: This DNF represents the XOR (exclusive OR) function!

DNF vs CNF: When to Use Which?

Use DNF When:

  • Expression has few TRUE rows in truth table
  • Building from minterms
  • Implementing in hardware with OR gates at output
  • Database query optimization (OR of conditions)
  • More intuitive for "any of these conditions"

Use CNF When:

  • Expression has few FALSE rows in truth table
  • Building from maxterms
  • Input to SAT solvers (standard format)
  • Automated theorem proving
  • More intuitive for "all of these must be satisfied"

üìä Comparison: CNF vs DNF

Aspect CNF (Conjunctive Normal Form) DNF (Disjunctive Normal Form)
Structure AND of ORs OR of ANDs
Main Operator ‚àß (conjunction) ‚à® (disjunction)
Components Clauses (disjunctions) Terms (conjunctions)
From Truth Table Use FALSE rows (maxterms) Use TRUE rows (minterms)
Distribution Law Distribute OR over AND Distribute AND over OR
Common Use SAT solvers, theorem provers Circuit design, databases
Interpretation "All clauses must be satisfied" "At least one term must be satisfied"
Example (P ‚à® Q) ‚àß (¬¨P ‚à® R) (P ‚àß Q) ‚à® (¬¨P ‚àß R)

üîç Key Insight

CNF and DNF are duals of each other:

  • Swapping ‚àß with ‚à® in CNF gives you DNF structure
  • The conversion process is similar, just with different distribution laws
  • Both can represent any Boolean function
  • The "best" form depends on your application
