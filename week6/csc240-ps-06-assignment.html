Part A: Proof by Contradiction (40 points)

Problem 1 15 points
Prove by contradiction: √3 is irrational.
  • Follow the same structure as the √2 irrationality proof from Module 6.2
  • Assume √3 = a/b where gcd(a,b) = 1 (lowest terms)
  • You'll need to prove: if 3 | n², then 3 | n
  • The contradiction arises when both a and b are divisible by 3
Criterion Points
Correct assumption setup (√3 = a/b in lowest terms) 3
Correct algebraic manipulation (3b² = a²) 3
Proves 3|a² implies 3|a (with justification) 4
Shows both a and b divisible by 3 3
Clear statement of contradiction and conclusion 2
Problem 2 12 points
Prove by contradiction: There is no largest even integer.
  • Assume there exists a largest even integer N
  • Construct a larger even integer from N
  • Consider N + 2 and show it's even and larger than N
  • This contradicts N being the largest
Problem 3 13 points
Prove by contradiction: If n² is odd, then n is odd.
  • This can also be done by contrapositive, but use contradiction as specified
  • Assume n² is odd AND n is even
  • If n is even, write n = 2k and compute n²
  • Show n² must be even, contradicting the assumption

Part B: Mathematical Induction (60 points)

Problem 4 20 points
Prove by mathematical induction: For all n ≥ 1,
1² + 2² + 3² + ... + n² = n(n+1)(2n+1)/6
  • Base case: Verify for n = 1 that 1² = 1(2)(3)/6
  • Inductive step: Assume true for k, prove for k+1
  • Split: (sum to k+1) = (sum to k) + (k+1)²
  • Apply IH to replace sum to k with k(k+1)(2k+1)/6
  • Find common denominator and factor to get (k+1)(k+2)(2k+3)/6
Criterion Points
Correct base case verification 4
Clear statement of inductive hypothesis 3
Correct setup of what to prove (P(k+1)) 3
Correctly applies IH in derivation 4
Correct algebraic manipulation 4
Clear conclusion citing induction 2
Problem 5 20 points
Prove by mathematical induction: For all n ≥ 0, 5 divides (6ⁿ - 1).
  • Base case: Check that 5 | (6⁰ - 1) = 5 | 0
  • Inductive hypothesis: 5 | (6ᵏ - 1), so 6ᵏ - 1 = 5m for some integer m
  • Key manipulation: 6ᵏ⁺¹ - 1 = 6·6ᵏ - 1 = 6·6ᵏ - 6 + 5 = 6(6ᵏ - 1) + 5
  • Apply IH and factor out 5
Problem 6 20 points
Prove by mathematical induction: For all n ≥ 1, 2ⁿ > n.
  • Base case: Verify 2¹ > 1
  • Inductive hypothesis: Assume 2ᵏ > k for some k ≥ 1
  • Goal: Show 2ᵏ⁺¹ > k + 1
  • Key insight: 2ᵏ⁺¹ = 2·2ᵏ > 2k (by IH)
  • Show 2k ≥ k + 1 when k ≥ 1

Common Mistakes to Avoid

⚠️ Watch Out For:

  • Contradiction: Forgetting to state the assumption at the beginning
  • Contradiction: Not clearly identifying what the contradiction is
  • Induction: Skipping the base case or not verifying it explicitly
  • Induction: Not using the inductive hypothesis in your proof
  • Induction: Confusing what you're assuming (P(k)) with what you're proving (P(k+1))
  • Both: Skipping algebraic steps that are crucial to the argument
