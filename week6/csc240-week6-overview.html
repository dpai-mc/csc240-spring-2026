📋 Learning Objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

🔴 Proof by Contradiction

  • Explain the logical foundation of proof by contradiction using the law of excluded middle
  • Identify when proof by contradiction is the most appropriate technique
  • Construct well-structured contradiction proofs for existence and uniqueness claims
  • Apply proof by contradiction to prove classic results (irrationality of √2, infinitude of primes)
  • Distinguish between proof by contradiction and proof by contrapositive

🔵 Mathematical Induction Introduction

  • Explain the principle of mathematical induction and why it produces valid proofs
  • Identify the two essential components: base case and inductive step
  • Write complete induction proofs for summation formulas
  • Apply induction to prove divisibility properties and inequalities
  • Connect mathematical induction to the well-ordering principle
  • Recognize the relationship between induction and recursive algorithm correctness

📚 Prerequisites from Week 5

Before beginning Week 6, ensure mastery of these concepts from Week 5:

Direct Proof Technique Foundation for the inductive step in induction proofs. Assume hypothesis, derive conclusion.
Proof by Contrapositive Essential for understanding the distinction from contradiction. Proves ¬Q → ¬P to establish P → Q.
Counterexamples Helps identify which claims require induction vs. those that are simply false.
Mathematical Writing — The 4 C's Clarity, Completeness, Correctness, Conciseness. All proofs this week require clear, well-structured writing.
Logical Equivalences Especially (P → Q) ≡ (¬Q → ¬P). Required for setting up contradiction and contrapositive arguments.
Quantifier Negation ¬(∀x, P(x)) ≡ ∃x, ¬P(x). Essential for formulating the negation in contradiction proofs.
Definitions (Even, Odd, Divisibility) These fundamental definitions are used throughout induction and contradiction proofs this week.

⚠️ Common Errors to Avoid

These are the most frequent mistakes students make on proof assignments. Learn them now to avoid losing points.

Error Example Correction
Not clearly stating the assumption Starting proof without "Assume ¬P" Always explicitly state what you're assuming at the outset
Deriving something false but not a contradiction "This gives a negative number" Explain WHY this is impossible — connect it to a known truth
Confusing with contrapositive Proving ¬Q → ¬P and calling it contradiction Contradiction requires deriving Q ∧ ¬Q from ¬P — not just ¬P
Not using the assumption Proof doesn't reference the negated statement at all Every step in your derivation must depend on the assumed ¬P
Error Example Correction
Missing base case Jumping straight to the inductive step Always verify the base case explicitly — it's not optional
Wrong base case value Using n = 1 when the theorem starts at n = 0 Check what values the theorem actually covers — read carefully
Not using the inductive hypothesis Proving P(k+1) entirely from scratch Your proof MUST explicitly reference and apply P(k)
Circular reasoning "Assume P(k+1) is true..." You assume P(k), then PROVE P(k+1) — never assume P(k+1)
Algebra errors Incorrect expansion, factoring mistakes Double-check every algebraic step — show all intermediate work
Not stating IH clearly Vague reference to "the formula" Write out exactly what P(k) says — no abbreviations

💡 Tips for Success

For Contradiction Proofs

  • Clearly state your assumption at the very beginning of the proof
  • Keep track of what you're trying to contradict throughout the derivation
  • Make sure your contradiction is genuine — not just "this seems odd"
  • Use definitions and known theorems to derive consequences from ¬P

For Induction Proofs

  • Write out P(n) explicitly before starting — know exactly what you're proving
  • Verify the base case carefully — don't assume it's obvious
  • State the inductive hypothesis clearly — write out exactly what P(k) says
  • Explicitly use the IH — your derivation must reference it by name
  • Show your algebra — never skip steps in an induction proof

General Proof Writing

  • Plan before writing — know where you're going before you start
  • Use the standard templates from the modules — they provide essential structure
  • Check your work — verify base cases, double-check all algebra
  • Read your proof aloud — if it doesn't flow logically when spoken, revise it
  • Seek help early — don't wait until the last day to start challenging proofs

Week 7 Preview: Advanced Induction Techniques

Strong Induction

Assume P(n₀), P(n₀+1), …, P(k) are ALL true when proving P(k+1) — more powerful for some problems.

Structural Induction

Induction on recursively-defined structures — trees, linked lists, parse trees, and other data structures.

Well-Ordering Principle

Deeper connections between induction and the well-ordering principle of natural numbers.

Applications

Prime factorization, Fibonacci properties, recursive data structure analysis, and recurrence relations.
