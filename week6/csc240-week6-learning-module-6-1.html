📚 Introduction

Welcome to Module 6.1! In Week 5, you learned about direct proofs and proof by contrapositive. This week, we introduce two powerful new proof techniques: proof by contradiction and mathematical induction. This module focuses on the foundations of proof by contradiction.

Proof by contradiction is one of the most elegant and powerful tools in a mathematician's arsenal. Rather than directly proving a statement is true, we assume it's false and show that this assumption leads to an impossibility—a contradiction. When we reach this logical dead end, we know our assumption must have been wrong, confirming the original statement is true.

  • Identify when proof by contradiction is the appropriate technique

🔬 The Logical Foundation

Why Does Proof by Contradiction Work?

The validity of proof by contradiction rests on a fundamental principle of classical logic: the Law of Excluded Middle. This law states that for any proposition P, either P is true or ¬P (not P) is true—there is no middle ground.

📖 Law of Excluded Middle

For any proposition P: P ∨ ¬P is always true (a tautology).

A statement is either true or false; there is no third option.

Combined with another principle—the Law of Non-Contradiction—we have the logical basis for our proof technique:

📖 Law of Non-Contradiction

For any proposition P: ¬(P ∧ ¬P) is always true.

A statement cannot be both true and false simultaneously.

The Core Argument

Here's how these laws combine to make proof by contradiction valid:

If assuming ¬P leads to a contradiction (Q ∧ ¬Q), then ¬P must be false, so P must be true.

In symbolic logic, we're using this valid argument form:

(¬P → (Q ∧ ¬Q)) → P

This reads: "If not-P implies a contradiction, then P is true."

💡 Key Insight

A contradiction is an impossible statement—something that is always false. Since valid logical reasoning cannot lead from a true premise to a false conclusion, if our assumption leads to a contradiction, the assumption itself must be false.

🏗️ Structure of a Contradiction Proof

Every proof by contradiction follows a specific template. Understanding this structure is crucial for both writing and reading these proofs.

📋 Standard Structure Template

  1. State what you want to prove: "We will prove that P is true."
  2. Assume the negation: "Suppose, for the sake of contradiction, that ¬P."
  3. Derive logical consequences: Use the assumption along with known facts, definitions, and previously proven theorems.
  4. Arrive at a contradiction: Show that the consequences lead to something impossible (Q ∧ ¬Q).
  5. Conclude: "This is a contradiction. Therefore, our assumption ¬P must be false, so P is true. ∎"

Identifying the Contradiction

The "contradiction" in step 4 can take several forms:

Common Types of Contradictions

  • Direct logical contradiction: Deriving both Q and ¬Q for some statement Q
  • Contradicting a known fact: Deriving something false (like 1 = 0 or 2 is odd)
  • Contradicting a premise: Deriving something that contradicts a given condition
  • Contradicting the assumption: Deriving P from the assumption ¬P

Key Phrases to Recognize

When reading proofs, these phrases signal a proof by contradiction:

Signal Phrases

  • "Suppose, for the sake of contradiction, that..."
  • "Assume, to the contrary, that..."
  • "We proceed by contradiction. Suppose..."
  • "Assume not. Then..."
  • "By way of contradiction, assume..."

🎯 When to Use Proof by Contradiction

While proof by contradiction is powerful, it's not always the best choice. Understanding when to apply this technique is as important as knowing how to use it.

Ideal Scenarios for Contradiction

✅ Use Contradiction When:

  • Proving impossibility or non-existence: "There is no X such that..." or "It is impossible for..."
  • The negation gives you something concrete to work with: Negating "there is no largest integer" gives you "there exists a largest integer N"
  • Direct proof seems difficult: When you can't see a clear path forward
  • Proving uniqueness: "There is exactly one X" (assume two exist)
  • Dealing with irrational numbers: Classic application area

⚠️ Consider Other Methods When:

  • A direct proof is straightforward: Don't overcomplicate things
  • The statement is an implication (P → Q): Contrapositive might be cleaner
  • You need to construct something: Contradiction proves existence but doesn't build

Contradiction vs. Contrapositive

Students often confuse these two techniques. Here's a clear comparison:

Aspect Contrapositive Contradiction
Statement type Implications (P → Q) Any statement
What you assume ¬Q (negation of conclusion) ¬(whole statement)
What you prove ¬P (negation of hypothesis) A contradiction
Logical basis (P → Q) ≡ (¬Q → ¬P) (¬P → F) → P

Example Comparison

Statement: If n² is even, then n is even.

Contrapositive approach:

  • Assume n is odd
  • Prove n² is odd

Contradiction approach:

  • Assume n² is even AND n is odd
  • Derive a contradiction

For this statement, contrapositive is cleaner because we just need to show odd × odd = odd.

📝 Worked Example: No Largest Integer

Let's work through our first complete proof by contradiction in detail, examining each step carefully.

📖 Theorem

There is no largest integer.

Step-by-Step Analysis

Step 1: Understand the statement.

We need to prove that no integer can be the largest. In other words, for every integer, there exists a larger one.

Step 2: Form the negation.

The negation of "there is no largest integer" is "there exists a largest integer."

This gives us something concrete: we can name this supposed largest integer and call it N.

Step 3: Set up the assumption.

Suppose, for the sake of contradiction, that there exists a largest integer N.

This means: for all integers m, we have m ≤ N.

Step 4: Derive consequences.

Consider the number N + 1.

  • Since N is an integer, N + 1 is also an integer (closure property)
  • By basic arithmetic, N + 1 > N
Step 5: Identify the contradiction.

We have:

  • N is the largest integer (our assumption)
  • N + 1 is an integer larger than N (what we derived)

These cannot both be true. This is a contradiction!

Step 6: State the conclusion.

Since our assumption led to a contradiction, the assumption must be false. Therefore, there is no largest integer. ∎

The Complete Proof (Written Form)

Proof

Suppose, for the sake of contradiction, that there exists a largest integer N. Then for all integers m, we have m ≤ N.

Consider N + 1. Since the integers are closed under addition, N + 1 is an integer. Moreover, N + 1 > N.

This contradicts our assumption that N is the largest integer.

Therefore, there is no largest integer. ∎

💡 Why This Works

The key insight is that assuming a largest integer exists gives us a specific object (N) to work with. We can then use properties of integers to construct something that violates what N is supposed to be.

📝 Worked Example: Rational + Irrational = Irrational

This example shows how contradiction proofs work with number properties.

📖 Theorem

The sum of a rational number and an irrational number is irrational.

Background: Rational Numbers

Before we begin, let's recall the definition:

📖 Definition: Rational Number

A number r is rational if it can be written as r = a/b where a and b are integers and b ≠ 0.

A number is irrational if it is not rational—it cannot be expressed as a ratio of integers.

Key Property of Rationals

Rational numbers are closed under addition and subtraction: if r and s are rational, then r + s and r − s are also rational.

The Proof

Proof

Let r be a rational number and let x be an irrational number. We will prove that r + x is irrational.

Suppose, for the sake of contradiction, that r + x is rational.

Then r + x = a/b for some integers a, b with b ≠ 0.

Since r is rational, we can write r = c/d for some integers c, d with d ≠ 0.

Now, solving for x:

x = (r + x) − r = a/b − c/d = (ad − bc)/(bd)

Since a, b, c, d are integers with b ≠ 0 and d ≠ 0:

  • ad − bc is an integer
  • bd is an integer and bd ≠ 0

Therefore, x is the ratio of two integers with nonzero denominator, which means x is rational.

But this contradicts our premise that x is irrational!

Therefore, our assumption must be false, and r + x is irrational. ∎

Analyzing the Proof Structure

What did we assume? ▼

We assumed the negation of what we wanted to prove. We wanted to prove "r + x is irrational," so we assumed "r + x is rational."

What was the contradiction? ▼

We derived that x is rational, but x was given to be irrational. This is a direct contradiction of a premise.

What was the key insight? ▼

The key insight was using the closure property: if r + x were rational, then (r + x) − r would also be rational. But (r + x) − r = x, so x would be rational—contradiction!

💻 Connection to Computer Science

Proof by contradiction isn't just theoretical—it has direct applications in computer science, particularly in proving algorithm impossibility and establishing lower bounds.

Application 1: Proving Algorithm Limitations

Contradiction proofs are essential for showing that certain problems cannot be solved efficiently or at all.

Example: Halting Problem (Preview)

One of the most famous results in computer science—that no program can determine whether an arbitrary program will halt—is proved by contradiction. The proof assumes such a program exists and derives a logical paradox.

Application 2: Loop Invariant Verification

When proving program correctness, we sometimes use contradiction to show that certain states are impossible.

Example: Array Bounds

To prove an array access is always valid, we might assume (for contradiction) that an out-of-bounds access occurs, then show this contradicts our loop invariant.

Java Code Example: Defensive Programming

Consider this method that relies on a contradiction-based argument:

/**
 * Returns the index of the minimum element in a non-empty array.
 * 
 * We can prove this method is correct: if no minimum existed,
 * that would contradict the well-ordering of integers.
 */
public static int findMinIndex(int[] arr) {
    // Precondition: array must be non-empty
    if (arr == null || arr.length == 0) {
        throw new IllegalArgumentException("Array must be non-empty");
    }
    
    int minIndex = 0;
    
    // Loop invariant: arr[minIndex] ≤ arr[i] for all i < current index
    for (int i = 1; i < arr.length; i++) {
        if (arr[i] < arr[minIndex]) {
            minIndex = i;
        }
    }
    
    // Postcondition: arr[minIndex] is the minimum
    // Proof sketch (by contradiction):
    // Suppose arr[minIndex] is NOT the minimum.
    // Then there exists j where arr[j] < arr[minIndex].
    // But our loop checked all indices, so this is impossible.
    // Contradiction! Therefore arr[minIndex] IS the minimum.
    
    return minIndex;
}

Application 3: Proving Uniqueness

Contradiction is perfect for proving something is unique:

/**
 * Binary search returns the unique index where key would be inserted
 * to maintain sorted order (if key is not found).
 * 
 * Uniqueness proof (by contradiction):
 * Suppose there are two valid insertion points i and j where i < j.
 * Then arr[i-1] < key < arr[i] and arr[j-1] < key < arr[j].
 * But arr[i] ≤ arr[j-1] (array is sorted), so key < arr[i] ≤ arr[j-1] < key.
 * This means key < key, a contradiction!
 */
public static int binarySearchInsertionPoint(int[] arr, int key) {
    int low = 0;
    int high = arr.length;
    
    while (low < high) {
        int mid = low + (high - low) / 2;
        if (arr[mid] < key) {
            low = mid + 1;
        } else {
            high = mid;
        }
    }
    
    return low;
}

💡 Key Takeaway

In computer science, contradiction proofs help us:

  • Prove that algorithms are correct for all inputs
  • Show that certain problems have no solution
  • Establish that solutions are unique
  • Verify that error states are unreachable

Problem 1: No Smallest Positive Rational

Problem Statement ▼

Prove: There is no smallest positive rational number.

Hint: This is similar to the "no largest integer" proof. What can you do to any positive rational to get a smaller one?

Solution ▼

Proof

Suppose, for the sake of contradiction, that there exists a smallest positive rational number r.

Consider r/2. Since r is rational, r = a/b for integers a, b with b ≠ 0 and a > 0 (since r is positive).

Then r/2 = a/(2b), which is also rational (ratio of integers with nonzero denominator).

Since r > 0, we have r/2 > 0, so r/2 is a positive rational.

But r/2 < r, which contradicts our assumption that r is the smallest positive rational.

Therefore, there is no smallest positive rational number. ∎

Problem 2: Odd Sum Implies Odd Addend

Problem Statement ▼

Prove: If the sum of two integers is odd, then at least one of them is odd.

Hint: What happens if you assume both are even?

Solution ▼

Proof

Let a and b be integers such that a + b is odd.

Suppose, for the sake of contradiction, that neither a nor b is odd—that is, both a and b are even.

Then a = 2m and b = 2n for some integers m and n.

Therefore, a + b = 2m + 2n = 2(m + n).

Since m + n is an integer, a + b is even.

But this contradicts our premise that a + b is odd.

Therefore, at least one of a or b must be odd. ∎

Problem 3: Unique Zero

Problem Statement ▼

Prove: There is exactly one real number x such that x + x = x.

Hint: First find what x must be, then prove it's unique by contradiction.

Solution ▼

Proof

Existence: x = 0 satisfies x + x = x since 0 + 0 = 0. ✓

Uniqueness (by contradiction):

Suppose there exist two distinct real numbers a and b such that a + a = a and b + b = b.

From a + a = a, we get 2a = a, so a = 0.

From b + b = b, we get 2b = b, so b = 0.

But then a = b = 0, contradicting our assumption that a and b are distinct.

Therefore, x = 0 is the unique solution. ∎
