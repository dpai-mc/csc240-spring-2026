1   The Handshaking Lemma

1.1   Statement

Theorem — Handshaking Lemma

For any undirected graph $$G = (V, E)$$,

$$\sum_{v \in V} \deg(v) \;=\; 2\,|E|$$

In words: the sum of the degrees of all vertices equals twice the number of edges.

1.2   Intuition

Why does this work? Consider any single edge $$\{u, v\}$$. It is incident to vertex $$u$$ (adding 1 to $$\deg(u)$$) and incident to vertex $$v$$ (adding 1 to $$\deg(v)$$). So every edge contributes exactly 2 to the total degree sum — once for each endpoint. Since each of the $$|E|$$ edges contributes 2, the total is $$2|E|$$.

Think of a handshake: every handshake involves two hands. If you count the total number of hands involved in all handshakes, you get twice the number of handshakes.

1.3   Proof Sketch (Direct Proof)

Show proof sketch
Proof (Direct)

Goal: Show that $$\displaystyle\sum_{v \in V} \deg(v) = 2|E|$$.

Step 1. Define a counting function. For each edge $$e \in E$$ and each vertex $$v \in V$$, let

$$ f(v, e) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } e \text{ is incident to } v \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} $$

Step 2. Count by vertex. For a fixed vertex $$v$$,

$$\sum_{e \in E} f(v, e) = \deg(v)$$

because $$f(v,e) = 1$$ exactly for the edges incident to $$v$$.

Step 3. Sum over all vertices:

$$\sum_{v \in V}\sum_{e \in E} f(v, e) = \sum_{v \in V} \deg(v)$$

Step 4. Count by edge. For a fixed edge $$e = \{u, w\}$$,

$$\sum_{v \in V} f(v, e) = 2$$

because $$e$$ is incident to exactly its two endpoints $$u$$ and $$w$$.

Step 5. Sum over all edges:

$$\sum_{e \in E}\sum_{v \in V} f(v, e) = \sum_{e \in E} 2 = 2|E|$$

Step 6. The double sums in Steps 3 and 5 count the same quantity (just in different order), so:

$$\sum_{v \in V} \deg(v) = 2|E| \qquad \blacksquare$$

This is a direct proof — the same technique you practiced in Week 5. We assumed nothing beyond the definition of a graph and counted the same set of incidences two ways.

1.4   Corollary: Even Number of Odd-Degree Vertices

Corollary

In any undirected graph, the number of vertices with odd degree is even.

Why is this true?

The Handshaking Lemma says the total degree sum is $$2|E|$$, which is even. Partition the vertices into two groups: those with even degree and those with odd degree.

$$\underbrace{\sum_{\text{even-deg vertices}} \deg(v)}_{\text{even}} + \underbrace{\sum_{\text{odd-deg vertices}} \deg(v)}_{\text{must also be even}} = 2|E| \;(\text{even})$$

The first group's sum is even (a sum of even numbers). For the total to be even, the second group's sum must also be even. A sum of odd numbers is even only when there is an even count of them. Therefore the number of odd-degree vertices is even.

1.5   Worked Examples

✍ Example 1 — Edge Count from a Degree List

Problem: A graph has five vertices with degrees 3, 3, 2, 2, 4. How many edges does it have?

Solution:

$$\sum \deg(v) = 3 + 3 + 2 + 2 + 4 = 14$$ $$|E| = \frac{14}{2} = 7$$

The graph has 7 edges.

✍ Example 2 — Testing a Proposed Degree Sequence

Problem: Can a graph on 5 vertices have degree sequence (3, 3, 3, 3, 1)?

Solution:

$$\sum \deg(v) = 3 + 3 + 3 + 3 + 1 = 13$$

Since 13 is odd, the Handshaking Lemma says no such graph can exist. The degree sum must be even ($$2|E|$$).

2   Degree Sequences

2.1   Definition

Definition — Degree Sequence

The degree sequence of a graph $$G$$ is the list of vertex degrees arranged in non-increasing order. For example, if a graph has vertices with degrees 2, 4, 3, 2, 3, its degree sequence is $$(4, 3, 3, 2, 2)$$.

2.2   The Even-Sum Necessary Condition

From the Handshaking Lemma, a sequence of non-negative integers $$(d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_n)$$ can be the degree sequence of a graph only if the sum $$d_1 + d_2 + \cdots + d_n$$ is even. This is a necessary condition but not always sufficient.

2.3   The Erdős–Gallai Theorem (Stated Without Proof)

🔑 Erdős–Gallai Theorem (For Awareness)

A non-increasing sequence of non-negative integers $$(d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_n)$$ is the degree sequence of a simple graph if and only if the sum is even and for each $$k = 1, 2, \ldots, n$$:

$$\sum_{i=1}^{k} d_i \;\leq\; k(k-1) + \sum_{i=k+1}^{n} \min(d_i, k)$$

You do not need to memorize or prove this theorem. It is included so you know that a complete characterization exists. For this course, the even-sum test (Handshaking Lemma) is your primary tool, and you can verify small cases by attempting to construct a graph.

2.4   Worked Example — Verify and Construct

✍ Example 3 — Is (4, 3, 3, 2, 2) Realizable?

Step 1 — Even-sum test: $$4 + 3 + 3 + 2 + 2 = 14$$. Since 14 is even, the sequence might be realizable.

Step 2 — Attempt construction: Label the vertices $$a, b, c, d, e$$ with target degrees 4, 3, 3, 2, 2.

  • Vertex $$a$$ needs degree 4 — connect $$a$$ to all other vertices: $$\{a,b\}, \{a,c\}, \{a,d\}, \{a,e\}$$. Now $$\deg(a) = 4$$ ✓
  • Remaining needs: $$b$$ needs 2 more, $$c$$ needs 2 more, $$d$$ needs 1 more, $$e$$ needs 1 more.
  • Connect $$b$$ to $$c$$ and $$d$$: $$\{b,c\}, \{b,d\}$$. Now $$\deg(b) = 3$$ ✓, $$\deg(c) = 2$$ (needs 1 more), $$\deg(d) = 2$$ ✓
  • Connect $$c$$ to $$e$$: $$\{c,e\}$$. Now $$\deg(c) = 3$$ ✓, $$\deg(e) = 2$$ ✓

Result: $$E = \{\{a,b\}, \{a,c\}, \{a,d\}, \{a,e\}, \{b,c\}, \{b,d\}, \{c,e\}\}$$ — that is 7 edges, matching $$14/2 = 7$$. The degree sequence (4, 3, 3, 2, 2) is realizable. ✓

3   Special Graph Families

Certain graph structures appear so frequently that they have standard names and well-known properties. Memorizing these families lets you instantly know edge counts, degree sequences, and connectivity — and they serve as benchmark examples when testing algorithms.

3.1   Reference Table

Table 1 — Special Graph Families
Graph Notation Vertices Edges Degree Sequence
Complete graph $$K_n$$ $$n$$ $$\binom{n}{2} = \frac{n(n-1)}{2}$$ All degrees $$= n - 1$$
Complete bipartite $$K_{m,n}$$ $$m + n$$ $$m \cdot n$$ $$m$$ vertices of degree $$n$$; $$n$$ vertices of degree $$m$$
Cycle $$C_n$$  ($$n \geq 3$$) $$n$$ $$n$$ All degrees $$= 2$$
Path $$P_n$$  ($$n \geq 1$$) $$n$$ $$n - 1$$ Two vertices of degree 1; rest degree 2
Wheel $$W_n$$  ($$n \geq 3$$) $$n + 1$$ $$2n$$ Hub has degree $$n$$; $$n$$ rim vertices each degree 3

3.2   Diagrams of Small Examples

$$K_5$$: 5 vertices, $$\binom{5}{2} = 10$$ edges, every vertex degree 4
$$K_{3,3}$$: 6 vertices (two groups of 3), $$3 \times 3 = 9$$ edges, degrees: $$(3,3,3,3,3,3)$$

3.3   Connecting $$K_n$$ to Counting (Week 7 Callback)

🔗 Connection to Week 7 — Combinations

The complete graph $$K_n$$ has one edge for every unordered pair of distinct vertices. Choosing 2 vertices from $$n$$ is exactly the combination $$\binom{n}{2}$$. So:

$$|E(K_n)| = \binom{n}{2} = \frac{n(n-1)}{2}$$

For $$K_5$$: $$\binom{5}{2} = 10$$ edges. For $$K_{10}$$: $$\binom{10}{2} = 45$$ edges. The counting tools from Week 7 give you instant edge counts for complete graphs.

3.4   Quick Verification with the Handshaking Lemma

You can cross-check every row in Table 1 using the Handshaking Lemma. For instance, $$W_n$$ has degree sequence with one hub of degree $$n$$ and $$n$$ rim vertices each of degree 3:

$$\sum \deg(v) = n + 3n = 4n = 2 \cdot 2n = 2|E| \;\checkmark$$

3.5   Recognition Exercise

Quick exercise: Which special graph has exactly 2 vertices of degree 1 and all other vertices of degree 2?

Answer: The path graph $$P_n$$. The two endpoints have degree 1, and every interior vertex has degree 2. No other special family in our table has this degree pattern.

4   Graph Isomorphism

4.1   Formal Definition

Definition — Graph Isomorphism

Graphs $$G_1 = (V_1, E_1)$$ and $$G_2 = (V_2, E_2)$$ are isomorphic, written $$G_1 \cong G_2$$, if there exists a bijection $$f : V_1 \to V_2$$ such that:

$$\{u, v\} \in E_1 \iff \{f(u), f(v)\} \in E_2$$

In words: there is a one-to-one relabeling of vertices that perfectly preserves all edges.

🔗 Connection to Week 2 — Bijections

The mapping $$f$$ is a bijection — the same concept you studied in Week 2 when classifying functions as injective, surjective, or bijective. A graph isomorphism is simply a bijection with the extra requirement that it preserves adjacency.

4.2   Necessary Invariants

If $$G_1 \cong G_2$$, then the two graphs must agree on all of the following properties (invariants):

  1. Same number of vertices: $$|V_1| = |V_2|$$
  2. Same number of edges: $$|E_1| = |E_2|$$
  3. Same degree sequence
  4. Same number of connected components
  5. Same cycle structure (same number of cycles of each length)
🔑 Key Insight

Invariants can disprove isomorphism (if any invariant differs, the graphs are not isomorphic), but invariants alone cannot prove isomorphism. Two non-isomorphic graphs can share all of the invariants above. To prove isomorphism, you must construct an explicit bijection and verify that it preserves all edges.

4.3   Worked Example — Isomorphic Pair

✍ Example 4 — Proving Isomorphism

Consider the following two graphs:

Graph $$G_1$$: $$V_1 = \{a, b, c, d\}$$, $$E_1 = \{\{a,b\}, \{b,c\}, \{c,d\}, \{d,a\}\}$$

Graph $$G_2$$: $$V_2 = \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$$, $$E_2 = \{\{1,3\}, \{3,2\}, \{2,4\}, \{4,1\}\}$$

Step 1 — Check invariants:

  • $$|V_1| = |V_2| = 4$$ ✓
  • $$|E_1| = |E_2| = 4$$ ✓
  • Degree sequences: both are $$(2, 2, 2, 2)$$ ✓
  • Both are connected with one 4-cycle ✓

Invariants match, so isomorphism is possible. We must now construct a bijection.

Step 2 — Construct bijection: Define $$f : V_1 \to V_2$$ as:

$$v$$$$f(v)$$
$$a$$$$1$$
$$b$$$$3$$
$$c$$$$2$$
$$d$$$$4$$

Step 3 — Verify edge preservation:

Edge in $$G_1$$Mapped edgeIn $$E_2$$?
$$\{a, b\}$$$$\{f(a), f(b)\} = \{1, 3\}$$✓
$$\{b, c\}$$$$\{f(b), f(c)\} = \{3, 2\}$$✓
$$\{c, d\}$$$$\{f(c), f(d)\} = \{2, 4\}$$✓
$$\{d, a\}$$$$\{f(d), f(a)\} = \{4, 1\}$$✓

Every edge maps correctly, and $$f$$ is a bijection. Therefore $$G_1 \cong G_2$$. ✓

4.4   Worked Example — Non-Isomorphic Pair

✍ Example 5 — Disproving Isomorphism

Consider two graphs, both with 5 vertices and 5 edges:

Graph $$H_1$$: $$C_5$$ — the cycle on 5 vertices. Degree sequence: $$(2, 2, 2, 2, 2)$$.

Graph $$H_2$$: A "bowtie-tail" graph — a triangle $$\{a,b,c\}$$ plus a path $$c\text{–}d\text{–}e$$. Edges: $$\{a,b\}, \{b,c\}, \{a,c\}, \{c,d\}, \{d,e\}$$. Degree sequence: $$(3, 2, 2, 2, 1)$$.

Comparison:

  • $$|V| = 5$$ for both ✓
  • $$|E| = 5$$ for both ✓
  • Degree sequence of $$H_1$$: $$(2, 2, 2, 2, 2)$$
  • Degree sequence of $$H_2$$: $$(3, 2, 2, 2, 1)$$

The degree sequences differ. Since degree sequence is an isomorphism invariant, $$H_1 \not\cong H_2$$. No bijection can map a degree-3 vertex in $$H_2$$ to any vertex in $$H_1$$ (all of which have degree 2).

4.5   A Note on Computational Complexity

⚠ Complexity Note

Graph isomorphism is one of the few natural problems whose exact computational complexity is unknown. It is clearly in NP (a proposed bijection can be verified in polynomial time), but it has not been proven to be NP-complete, nor has a polynomial-time algorithm been found for the general case. In 2015, László Babai announced a quasipolynomial-time algorithm, but the problem's exact classification remains open.

For this course, you will work with small graphs where you can test invariants and construct bijections by hand. The key takeaway: invariants give you efficient screening, but a constructive bijection is the only way to confirm isomorphism.

Check 12.4a — Handshaking Lemma Application

A graph has 8 vertices, each of degree 3. How many edges does it have, and is it a named special graph from Section 3?

✔ Correct. By the Handshaking Lemma: $$\sum \deg(v) = 8 \times 3 = 24 = 2|E|$$, so $$|E| = 12$$. Checking the named families: $$K_8$$ would require degree 7 (not 3). $$C_8$$ has degree 2 (not 3). $$P_8$$ has two degree-1 vertices. $$K_{4,4}$$ has 16 edges and degree 4. $$W_7$$ has a hub of degree 7. None match. The graph is 3-regular (every vertex has degree 3), but it is not one of our five named families.

Now check each named family. $$K_8$$ requires every vertex to have degree 7. $$C_8$$ has all degrees equal to 2. $$K_{4,4}$$ has $$4 \times 4 = 16$$ edges and all degrees equal to 4. None of the five named families produce an 8-vertex graph where every vertex has degree 3. The correct answer is C.

Check 12.4b — Special Graph Properties

How many edges does $$K_5$$ have, and how many edges does $$K_{3,3}$$ have?

✔ Correct. $$K_5$$: $$\binom{5}{2} = \frac{5 \times 4}{2} = 10$$ edges, degree sequence $$(4,4,4,4,4)$$. $$K_{3,3}$$: $$3 \times 3 = 9$$ edges, degree sequence $$(3,3,3,3,3,3)$$. You can verify both with the Handshaking Lemma: for $$K_5$$, $$\sum \deg = 5 \times 4 = 20 = 2 \times 10$$ ✓. For $$K_{3,3}$$, $$\sum \deg = 6 \times 3 = 18 = 2 \times 9$$ ✓.
✘ Not quite. Recall the formulas: $$K_n$$ has $$\binom{n}{2}$$ edges, so $$K_5$$ has $$\binom{5}{2} = 10$$. $$K_{m,n}$$ has $$m \cdot n$$ edges, so $$K_{3,3}$$ has $$3 \times 3 = 9$$. The correct answer is B.

Check 12.4c — Isomorphism Determination

Two graphs are shown below. Both have 4 vertices and 4 edges.

Graph $$R$$: $$V = \{1,2,3,4\}$$, $$E = \{\{1,2\}, \{2,3\}, \{3,4\}, \{4,1\}\}$$ — a 4-cycle.

Graph $$S$$: $$V = \{a,b,c,d\}$$, $$E = \{\{a,b\}, \{a,c\}, \{a,d\}, \{b,c\}\}$$ — a "fan" shape.

Are $$R$$ and $$S$$ isomorphic?

✔ Correct. Both graphs have 4 vertices and 4 edges, so those invariants match. But the degree sequences differ. In $$R$$, every vertex has degree 2 → sequence $$(2,2,2,2)$$. In $$S$$, vertex $$a$$ has degree 3 and vertex $$d$$ has degree 1 → sequence $$(3,2,2,1)$$. Since degree sequence is an isomorphism invariant, $$R \not\cong S$$. This illustrates the key insight: matching vertex and edge counts is not sufficient — you must also check degree sequences (and potentially other invariants).
✘ Not quite. Compute the degree of each vertex. In $$R$$: $$\deg(1)=2, \deg(2)=2, \deg(3)=2, \deg(4)=2$$. In $$S$$: $$\deg(a)=3, \deg(b)=2, \deg(c)=2, \deg(d)=1$$. The degree sequences are $$(2,2,2,2)$$ vs. $$(3,2,2,1)$$ — they don't match. Since degree sequence must be preserved by any isomorphism, these graphs are not isomorphic. The correct answer is B.

Check 12.4d — Degree Sequence Feasibility

Is the degree sequence $$(5, 3, 3, 3, 2, 2)$$ realizable as a simple graph? Select the best answer.

✔ Correct. First, $$5+3+3+3+2+2 = 18$$, which is even, so the Handshaking Lemma is satisfied. The graph would have $$18/2 = 9$$ edges. The maximum degree is 5, and with 6 vertices, the highest possible degree is 5 (connected to all other 5 vertices), so that is valid. We can construct such a graph: label the vertices $$v_1, \ldots, v_6$$ with target degrees $$5,3,3,3,2,2$$. Connect $$v_1$$ to all others (degree 5). Remaining needs: $$v_2$$ needs 2 more, $$v_3$$ needs 2 more, $$v_4$$ needs 2 more, $$v_5$$ needs 1 more, $$v_6$$ needs 1 more. Connect $$v_2\text{–}v_3$$, $$v_2\text{–}v_4$$, $$v_3\text{–}v_4$$, $$v_5\text{–}v_6$$. Check: $$\deg(v_2) = 3$$ ✓, $$\deg(v_3) = 3$$ ✓, $$\deg(v_4) = 3$$ ✓, $$\deg(v_5) = 2$$ ✓, $$\deg(v_6) = 2$$ ✓. Total edges: 5 + 4 = 9 ✓.
✘ Not quite. Check the sum: $$5+3+3+3+2+2 = 18$$, which is even — so the Handshaking Lemma does not rule it out. The maximum degree is 5, which is valid for a 6-vertex simple graph (a vertex can be adjacent to at most 5 others). You can verify realizability by constructing: connect the degree-5 vertex to all others, then distribute the remaining edges. The correct answer is C.

6   Module Summary

📚 Key Takeaways
  • Handshaking Lemma: $$\displaystyle\sum_{v \in V} \deg(v) = 2|E|$$. Each edge contributes 2 to the total degree. Corollary: the number of odd-degree vertices is always even.
  • Degree sequences must have an even sum to be realizable. The Erdős–Gallai theorem gives a full characterization, but for this course the even-sum test plus construction attempts are your primary tools.
  • Special graph families — $$K_n$$, $$K_{m,n}$$, $$C_n$$, $$P_n$$, $$W_n$$ — have known edge counts and degree sequences you can recall or derive instantly.
  • Graph isomorphism requires a bijection $$f: V_1 \to V_2$$ preserving adjacency. Invariants (vertex count, edge count, degree sequence, components, cycle structure) can disprove but never prove isomorphism.
