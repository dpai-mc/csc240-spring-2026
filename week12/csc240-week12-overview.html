Learning Goals for Week 12

By the end of this week you will be able to:

  1. Define fundamental graph terminology — vertex, edge, degree, path, cycle, connected graph, subgraph — and use these terms precisely in written and verbal descriptions.
  2. Distinguish among graph types — directed vs. undirected, weighted vs. unweighted, simple vs. multigraph — and identify when each type is appropriate for modeling a given problem.
  3. Construct adjacency matrix and adjacency list representations of a graph from a visual or verbal description, and convert between the two representations.
  4. Analyze space and time tradeoffs of adjacency matrix vs. adjacency list representations for sparse and dense graphs using Big-O notation.
  5. Apply the Handshaking Lemma to determine feasibility of degree sequences and solve related problems.
  6. Recognize and characterize special graph types — complete graphs Kₙ, complete bipartite graphs Kₘ,ₙ, cycles Cₙ, paths Pₙ, and wheels Wₙ.
  7. Determine whether two graphs are isomorphic by comparing invariants (vertex count, edge count, degree sequence) and identifying a bijection when one exists.

Key Concepts to Watch For

As you work through the modules, pay special attention to these foundational ideas. They appear repeatedly in Weeks 13–17 and on Quiz 4.

G = (V, E) Degree & deg(v) Adjacency Matrix Adjacency List Handshaking Lemma Directed vs. Undirected Weighted Graphs Graph Isomorphism Kₙ, Kₘ,ₙ, Cₙ, Pₙ, Wₙ
Quick concept preview
  • Graph G = (V, E): A set of vertices V and a set of edges E. Edges connect pairs of vertices. This simple definition is the starting point for everything.
  • Degree deg(v): The number of edges touching vertex v. For directed graphs, you will also track in-degree and out-degree separately.
  • Adjacency matrix vs. adjacency list: Two ways to store a graph in a computer. The matrix gives O(1) edge lookup but uses O(|V|²) space; the list uses O(|V| + |E|) space and is better for sparse graphs. Choosing the right one affects every algorithm you will write.
  • Handshaking Lemma: ∑(v ∈ V) deg(v) = 2|E|. Every edge contributes 2 to the total degree. This lets you verify or rule out proposed degree sequences instantly.
  • Graph isomorphism: Two graphs are isomorphic if you can relabel the vertices of one to get the other. You will compare invariants (vertex count, edge count, degree sequence) to rule out isomorphism, and construct an explicit bijection to prove it.

Next week you will learn Breadth-First Search and Depth-First Search — the two fundamental algorithms for exploring a graph.
