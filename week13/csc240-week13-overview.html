Week 13: Graph Traversal Algorithms

CSC 240 – Discrete Structures

Welcome & Weekly Focus

Welcome to Week 13! Last week you built the vocabulary and static representations of graphs — adjacency matrices, adjacency lists, and degree sequences. This week those structures come alive as we explore Breadth-First Search (BFS) and Depth-First Search (DFS), the two foundational algorithms that let us systematically visit every vertex and edge in a graph.

Graded Deliverable: Quiz 4 (60 pts) is the only graded item this week. It covers material from Weeks 11–13 (recursion, recurrence relations, graph fundamentals, and graph traversals).

Week 13 Learning Goals

By the end of this week, you should be able to:

BFS Fundamentals

  1. Explain how BFS explores a graph level by level using a queue, and hand-trace BFS on a given graph to produce a valid BFS tree.
  2. Describe why BFS guarantees shortest paths (fewest edges) in unweighted graphs and identify the BFS distance from a source to every reachable vertex.

DFS Fundamentals

  1. Explain how DFS explores a graph by going as deep as possible before backtracking, using either recursion or an explicit stack, and hand-trace DFS to produce discovery and finish timestamps.
  2. Classify edges encountered during DFS as tree, back, forward, or cross edges, and explain what each classification reveals about graph structure.

Comparing BFS and DFS

  1. Compare BFS and DFS in terms of data structure used, traversal order, time and space complexity, and the kinds of problems each is best suited for.

Traversal Applications

  1. Use BFS or DFS to identify all connected components of an undirected graph and determine whether a graph is connected.
  2. Apply DFS to perform topological sorting of a directed acyclic graph (DAG) and detect cycles in both directed and undirected graphs.

Connecting Theory and Practice

  1. Recognize how the recursive structure of DFS mirrors mathematical induction, and apply traversal algorithms to at least one real-world modeling scenario (e.g., social-network analysis, web crawling, or task scheduling).

Key Concepts to Watch For

  • BFS queue mechanics and shortest-path property — BFS processes vertices level by level; the first time it reaches a vertex is via the fewest possible edges.
  • DFS recursive structure, timestamps, and edge classification — Discovery and finish times reveal the nesting structure of the search, and each edge falls into exactly one category (tree, back, forward, or cross).
  • Connected components — Running BFS or DFS from every unvisited vertex partitions the graph into its connected pieces.
  • Topological sort and cycle detection — DFS finish times give a reverse topological order for DAGs; a back edge signals a cycle.
  • Choosing BFS vs. DFS — The right traversal depends on the problem: BFS for shortest paths, DFS for ordering and structural analysis.
  • DFS recursion and mathematical induction — The recursive "assume sub-problems are solved" pattern in DFS mirrors the inductive hypothesis you studied in Weeks 8–9.

How This Week Fits the Course

Looking back: Week 12 introduced graph definitions, types, and representations (adjacency matrices and lists). This week you add the algorithms that traverse those structures, turning static data into dynamic exploration.

Looking ahead: Week 14 builds directly on BFS and DFS with weighted-graph algorithms — Minimum Spanning Trees (Kruskal's and Prim's) and Dijkstra's shortest-path algorithm. Components 2 and 3 of the Final Project depend on the traversal skills you develop this week.

  • Recursion & Recurrences (Weeks 11–12): DFS is inherently recursive; its correctness arguments use the same inductive reasoning you practiced with recurrence relations.
  • Proof Techniques (Weeks 8–9): Proving BFS finds shortest paths and DFS correctly classifies edges relies on induction and contradiction.
  • Sets & Relations (Weeks 3–5): Graphs are built from sets of vertices and edges; adjacency is a relation. The language of sets and relations is the language of graph theory.
  • Algorithms & Complexity (Week 10): You will analyze BFS and DFS running times using the Big-O notation from Week 10.

Tips for Success This Week

  • Study the modules in order (13.1 → 13.2 → 13.3 → 13.4 → 13.5). Each module assumes you have completed the ones before it.
  • Hand-trace BFS and DFS on new graphs. Don't just re-read examples — draw a fresh graph, pick a start vertex, and work through the algorithm step by step with pencil and paper.
  • Use the Module 13.5 self-assessment checklist to gauge your readiness for Quiz 4 before you attempt it.
  • Quiz 4 covers all of Weeks 11–13. Set aside time to review recursion, recurrence relations, and graph representations in addition to this week's traversal material.
  • Try Lab 13 even though it is ungraded. Hands-on traversal practice is the single best way to prepare for both the quiz and the final project.
