What are Logical Equivalences?

In Week 3, you've learned how to construct truth tables and evaluate compound propositions. Now we explore a powerful concept: logical equivalence‚Äîwhen two different-looking expressions are actually the same!

üìñ Definition: Logical Equivalence

Two compound propositions p and q are logically equivalent if they have identical truth tables‚Äîthat is, they have the same truth value for every possible combination of their propositional variables.

Notation: We write p ‚â° q or p ‚áî q to indicate logical equivalence.

Alternative: p and q are logically equivalent if and only if p ‚Üî q is a tautology.

The Essential Logical Laws

These laws are the foundation for simplifying and transforming logical expressions. You should memorize these‚Äîthey're as fundamental as arithmetic rules!

Identity Laws

Identity Laws

Adding a tautology (T) or contradiction (F) in certain ways doesn't change the expression:

$$ p \land T \equiv p $$
$$ p \lor F \equiv p $$

Intuition:

  • "p AND TRUE" = p (TRUE doesn't restrict anything)
  • "p OR FALSE" = p (FALSE doesn't add anything)

Domination Laws

Domination Laws

Some combinations with T or F "dominate" the result:

$$ p \lor T \equiv T $$
$$ p \land F \equiv F $$

Intuition:

  • "p OR TRUE" = always TRUE (at least one is true!)
  • "p AND FALSE" = always FALSE (at least one is false!)

Idempotent Laws

Idempotent Laws

Repeating the same proposition doesn't change the result:

$$ p \land p \equiv p $$
$$ p \lor p \equiv p $$

Intuition:

  • "p AND p" = p (saying it twice doesn't matter)
  • "p OR p" = p (redundant disjunction)

Double Negation Law

Double Negation

Two negations cancel out:

$$ \neg\neg p \equiv p $$

Intuition: "NOT NOT p" = p (like a double negative in English)

Commutative Laws

Commutative Laws

Order doesn't matter for AND and OR:

$$ p \land q \equiv q \land p $$
$$ p \lor q \equiv q \lor p $$

Intuition: Just like addition: 2+3 = 3+2

Associative Laws

Associative Laws

Grouping doesn't matter for AND and OR:

$$ (p \land q) \land r \equiv p \land (q \land r) $$
$$ (p \lor q) \lor r \equiv p \lor (q \lor r) $$

Intuition: Like multiplication: (2√ó3)√ó4 = 2√ó(3√ó4)

Result: We can write $$ p \land q \land r $$ without parentheses!

Distributive Laws

Distributive Laws

AND distributes over OR, and OR distributes over AND:

$$ p \land (q \lor r) \equiv (p \land q) \lor (p \land r) $$
$$ p \lor (q \land r) \equiv (p \lor q) \land (p \lor r) $$

Note: The second law is different from arithmetic! In algebra, + doesn't distribute over √ó.

Absorption Laws

Absorption Laws

One proposition can "absorb" another:

$$ p \lor (p \land q) \equiv p $$
$$ p \land (p \lor q) \equiv p $$

Intuition:

  • $$ p \lor (p \land q) $$: If p is true, whole thing is true. If p is false, $$ (p \land q) $$ is false. So result = p.
  • $$ p \land (p \lor q) $$: If p is false, whole thing is false. If p is true, $$ (p \lor q) $$ is true. So result = p.

Negation Laws

Negation Laws (Complement Laws)

A proposition combined with its negation:

$$ p \lor \neg p \equiv T $$ (Law of Excluded Middle)
$$ p \land \neg p \equiv F $$ (Law of Contradiction)

Intuition:

  • $$ p \lor \neg p $$: Either p is true or it's not‚Äîalways true!
  • $$ p \land \neg p $$: p can't be both true and false‚Äîalways false!

üí° Example: Applying Laws to Simplify

Let's simplify: $$ (p \land T) \lor (p \land F) $$

Step Expression Law Applied
1 $$ (p \land T) \lor (p \land F) $$ Original expression
2 $$ p \lor (p \land F) $$ Identity Law: $$ p \land T \equiv p $$
3 $$ p \lor F $$ Domination Law: $$ p \land F \equiv F $$
4 $$ p $$ Identity Law: $$ p \lor F \equiv p $$

Result: $$ (p \land T) \lor (p \land F) \equiv p $$

The Most Important Equivalences

De Morgan's Laws are among the most useful logical equivalences, especially in computer science. They tell us how to negate compound propositions involving AND and OR.

üìñ De Morgan's Laws

$$ \neg(p \land q) \equiv \neg p \lor \neg q $$
$$ \neg(p \lor q) \equiv \neg p \land \neg q $$

In words:

  • NOT (p AND q) = (NOT p) OR (NOT q)
  • NOT (p OR q) = (NOT p) AND (NOT q)

Pattern: When you negate a compound statement:

  • Negate each component
  • Change AND to OR (or OR to AND)

Proving De Morgan's Laws with Truth Tables

Let's verify the first law using a truth table:

Observation: Columns 4 and 7 (highlighted) are identical!

Conclusion: $$\neg(p \land q) \equiv \neg p \lor \neg q$$ ‚úì

üí° Intuitive Understanding

Think about this statement: "It is NOT the case that (it's raining AND it's cold)"

This is true when:

  • It's not raining, OR
  • It's not cold, OR
  • Both (it's not raining and not cold)

In other words: "It's not raining OR it's not cold"

This is exactly what De Morgan's Law says!

De Morgan's Laws in Programming

üíª Example: Simplifying Code

Original code:

if (!(age >= 18 && hasLicense)) {
    System.out.println("Cannot drive");
}

Applying De Morgan's Law:

$$\neg(\text{age} \geq 18 \land \text{hasLicense}) \equiv \neg(\text{age} \geq 18) \lor \neg\text{hasLicense}$$

Which translates to: (age < 18) $$\lor$$ (!hasLicense)

Simplified code:

if (age < 18 || !hasLicense) {
    System.out.println("Cannot drive");
}

Result: Much clearer! No nested negation.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistake

WRONG: $$\neg(p \land q) \not\equiv \neg p \land \neg q$$

WRONG: $$\neg(p \lor q) \not\equiv \neg p \lor \neg q$$

Remember: You must FLIP the operator (AND ‚Üî OR) when distributing negation!

Extended De Morgan's Laws

De Morgan's Laws extend to more than two propositions:

$$ \neg(p_1 \land p_2 \land \cdots \land p_n) \equiv \neg p_1 \lor \neg p_2 \lor \cdots \lor \neg p_n $$
$$ \neg(p_1 \lor p_2 \lor \cdots \lor p_n) \equiv \neg p_1 \land \neg p_2 \land \cdots \land \neg p_n $$

üí° Example: Multiple Conditions

Statement: "It is NOT the case that (it's sunny AND warm AND dry)"

Equivalent: "It's not sunny OR it's not warm OR it's not dry"

Symbolically: $$\neg(p \land q \land r) \equiv \neg p \lor \neg q \lor \neg r$$

De Morgan's Laws and Set Theory

These laws have perfect analogues in set theory (from Week 1):

Logic

$$ \neg(p \land q) \equiv \neg p \lor \neg q $$
$$ \neg(p \lor q) \equiv \neg p \land \neg q $$

Set Theory

$$ (A \cap B)' = A' \cup B' $$
$$ (A \cup B)' = A' \cap B' $$

The parallel is exact: $$\land \leftrightarrow \cap$$, $$\lor \leftrightarrow \cup$$, $$\neg \leftrightarrow$$ complement!

Two Methods for Proving Equivalences

Method 1: Truth Tables

Process:

  1. Identify all variables
  2. Create 2‚Åø rows
  3. Evaluate both expressions
  4. Compare final columns

When to use: Small number of variables (‚â§3), or when you need to be absolutely sure

Method 2: Logical Laws

Process:

  1. Start with one expression
  2. Apply known equivalences
  3. Transform step-by-step
  4. Reach the other expression

When to use: Many variables, or when you want to show reasoning elegantly

Example 1: Proving with Truth Tables

üí° Prove: $$ p \to q \equiv \neg p \lor q $$

This is an important equivalence that shows implication can be rewritten using OR!

Significance: Every implication can be converted to OR form! This is useful in normal forms (Module 5).

Example 2: Proving with Logical Laws

üí° Prove: $$ \neg(p \to q) \equiv p \land \neg q $$

Let's prove this using known equivalences:

Step Expression Justification
1 $$ \neg(p \to q) $$ Start with left side
2 $$ \neg(\neg p \lor q) $$ Implication equivalence: $$ p \to q \equiv \neg p \lor q $$
3 $$ \neg\neg p \land \neg q $$ De Morgan's Law: $$ \neg(A \lor B) \equiv \neg A \land \neg B $$
4 $$ p \land \neg q $$ Double Negation: $$ \neg\neg p \equiv p $$

Conclusion: We've transformed the left side into the right side, so $$ \neg(p \to q) \equiv p \land \neg q $$ ‚úì

Interpretation: "NOT (if p then q)" means "p is true BUT q is false"‚Äîexactly when an implication fails!

‚ö†Ô∏è Proof Writing Guidelines

  • Start clearly: State what you're proving
  • One step at a time: Don't skip steps
  • Justify each step: Name the law you're using
  • Write neatly: Use proper notation
  • End clearly: State your conclusion with ‚úì or ‚àé

More Useful Equivalences to Know

Implication Equivalences

$$ p \to q \equiv \neg p \lor q $$
$$ p \to q \equiv \neg q \to \neg p $$ (Contrapositive)
$$ \neg(p \to q) \equiv p \land \neg q $$

Biconditional Equivalences

$$ p \leftrightarrow q \equiv (p \to q) \land (q \to p) $$
$$ p \leftrightarrow q \equiv (p \land q) \lor (\neg p \land \neg q) $$
$$ \neg(p \leftrightarrow q) \equiv p \oplus q $$

üí° Example: Multi-Step Proof

Prove: $$ (p \to q) \land (p \to r) \equiv p \to (q \land r) $$

1 $$ (p \to q) \land (p \to r) $$ Start with left side
2 $$ (\neg p \lor q) \land (\neg p \lor r) $$ Implication: $$ p \to q \equiv \neg p \lor q $$ (twice)
3 $$ \neg p \lor (q \land r) $$ Distributive Law: $$ (A \lor B) \land (A \lor C) \equiv A \lor (B \land C) $$
4 $$ p \to (q \land r) $$ Implication: $$ \neg p \lor X \equiv p \to X $$

Conclusion: $$ (p \to q) \land (p \to r) \equiv p \to (q \land r) $$ ‚úì

Interpretation: "If p then q, AND if p then r" is the same as "If p, then both q and r"

Strategy: When to Use Each Method

Use Truth Tables When:

  • You have 2-3 variables (manageable number of rows)
  • You're not sure which laws to apply
  • You need absolute certainty
  • You're checking your work from a law-based proof

Use Logical Laws When:

  • You can see a clear path using known equivalences
  • You want to show your reasoning process
  • You're writing a formal mathematical proof

What are Normal Forms?

A normal form is a standardized way of writing logical expressions. Just like you can write numbers in different forms (decimal, fraction, scientific notation), you can write logical expressions in different forms.

üìñ Literal

A literal is either a propositional variable (like p, q, r) or its negation (like ¬¨p, ¬¨q, ¬¨r).

Examples of literals: p, ¬¨p, q, ¬¨q, r, ¬¨r

NOT literals: $$ p \land q $$, $$ \neg\neg p $$, $$ p \lor q $$

Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF)

üìñ Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF)

A proposition is in DNF if it is a disjunction (OR) of one or more conjunctions (AND) of literals.

Pattern: (literal ‚àß literal ‚àß ...) ‚à® (literal ‚àß literal ‚àß ...) ‚à® ...

Think: "OR of ANDs"

üí° DNF Examples

In DNF:

  • $$ (p \land q) \lor (\neg p \land r) $$
  • $$ (p \land \neg q \land r) \lor (\neg p \land q) $$
  • $$ p \lor q $$ (each literal is a trivial conjunction)
  • $$ (p \land \neg p) \lor (q \land r) $$ (even if contradictory!)

NOT in DNF:

  • $$ (p \lor q) \land r $$ (AND is at the top level)
  • $$ p \land (q \lor r) $$ (OR inside an AND)
  • $$ \neg(p \land q) $$ (negation of compound expression)

Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF)

üìñ Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF)

A proposition is in CNF if it is a conjunction (AND) of one or more disjunctions (OR) of literals.

Pattern: (literal ‚à® literal ‚à® ...) ‚àß (literal ‚à® literal ‚à® ...) ‚àß ...

Think: "AND of ORs"

üí° CNF Examples

In CNF:

  • $$ (p \lor q) \land (\neg p \lor r) $$
  • $$ (p \lor \neg q \lor r) \land (\neg p \lor q) $$
  • $$ p \land q $$ (each literal is a trivial disjunction)
  • $$ (p \lor \neg p) \land (q \lor r) $$ (even if tautological!)

NOT in CNF:

  • $$ (p \land q) \lor r $$ (OR is at the top level)
  • $$ p \lor (q \land r) $$ (AND inside an OR)
  • $$ \neg(p \lor q) $$ (negation of compound expression)

Converting to Normal Forms

Here's a systematic process to convert any proposition to CNF or DNF:

Conversion Algorithm

  1. Eliminate implications and biconditionals:
    • Replace $$ p \to q $$ with $$ \neg p \lor q $$
    • Replace $$ p \leftrightarrow q $$ with $$ (p \land q) \lor (\neg p \land \neg q) $$
  2. Push negations inward:
    • Apply De Morgan's Laws: $$ \neg(p \land q) \to \neg p \lor \neg q $$
    • Apply Double Negation: $$ \neg\neg p \to p $$
    • Continue until negations apply only to variables
  3. Distribute to get desired form:
    • For DNF: Distribute ‚àß over ‚à® using: $$ p \land (q \lor r) \equiv (p \land q) \lor (p \land r) $$
    • For CNF: Distribute ‚à® over ‚àß using: $$ p \lor (q \land r) \equiv (p \lor q) \land (p \lor r) $$

üí° Example: Converting to DNF

Convert to DNF: $$ \neg(p \to q) $$

1 $$ \neg(p \to q) $$ Original expression
2 $$ \neg(\neg p \lor q) $$ Eliminate implication: $$ p \to q \equiv \neg p \lor q $$
3 $$ \neg\neg p \land \neg q $$ De Morgan's Law: $$ \neg(A \lor B) \equiv \neg A \land \neg B $$
4 $$ p \land \neg q $$ Double Negation: $$ \neg\neg p \equiv p $$

Result: $$ p \land \neg q $$ (This is in DNF! It's a single conjunction of literals)

üí° Example: Converting to CNF

Convert to CNF: $$ (p \lor q) \to r $$

1 $$ (p \lor q) \to r $$ Original expression
2 $$ \neg(p \lor q) \lor r $$ Eliminate implication: $$ A \to B \equiv \neg A \lor B $$
3 $$ (\neg p \land \neg q) \lor r $$ De Morgan's Law: $$ \neg(A \lor B) \equiv \neg A \land \neg B $$
4 $$ (\neg p \lor r) \land (\neg q \lor r) $$ Distribute ‚à® over ‚àß: $$ (A \land B) \lor C \equiv (A \lor C) \land (B \lor C) $$

Result: $$ (\neg p \lor r) \land (\neg q \lor r) $$ (This is in CNF! It's a conjunction of disjunctions)

Why Normal Forms Matter

üîç In Logic

  • Standard form for comparison
  • Easier to analyze properties
  • Foundation for automated reasoning
  • Used in theorem proving

üíª In Computer Science

  • Digital circuit design
  • SAT solvers (CNF)
  • Database query optimization
  • Compiler optimizations

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Note

Every proposition can be converted to both CNF and DNF, but:

  • The conversion may result in a much longer expression
  • DNF and CNF of the same proposition are usually different
  • Both forms are logically equivalent to the original

Programming Applications

Original code:

$$ p \lor T \equiv T $$

$$ p \land F \equiv F $$

Double Negation:

$$ \neg\neg p \equiv p $$

De Morgan's:

$$ \neg(p \land q) \equiv \neg p \lor \neg q $$

$$ \neg(p \lor q) \equiv \neg p \land \neg q $$

Distributive:

$$ p \land (q \lor r) \equiv (p \land q) \lor (p \land r) $$

$$ p \lor (q \land r) \equiv (p \lor q) \land (p \lor r) $$

Implication:

$$ p \to q \equiv \neg p \lor q $$
