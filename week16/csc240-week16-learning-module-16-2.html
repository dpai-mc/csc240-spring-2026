Where We Are
In Module 16.1, you learned to compute balance factors and identify when a BST violates the AVL property. Now you will learn the four rotation operations that restore balance. Mastering rotations is the key mechanical skill for AVL trees — Quiz 5 will include problems requiring you to identify the rotation type and draw the result.

1. When Rotations Are Needed

After an insertion or deletion in an AVL tree, one or more nodes along the path from the modified position back to the root may have their balance factors change. A rotation is triggered when:

Rotation Trigger Condition
A node's balance factor becomes +2 or −2 (i.e., |BF| = 2).

Identifying the Imbalanced Node

The imbalanced node (also called the pivot or critical node) is the lowest ancestor of the inserted or deleted node whose balance factor has become ±2. We fix the lowest violation first because correcting it often restores balance for all ancestors above it.

Recall from Module 16.1:

BF(node) = height(left subtree) − height(right subtree)
  • BF = +2 → the node is left-heavy (left subtree is too tall)
  • BF = −2 → the node is right-heavy (right subtree is too tall)

Once we identify the imbalanced node, we examine its heavy child (the child on the taller side) to determine which of the four rotation types to apply.

2. Rotation Decision Table

The rotation type is determined by two balance factors: that of the imbalanced node and that of its heavy child. The following table is your complete decision guide:

Imbalanced Node BF Heavy Child Heavy Child BF Case Name Rotation Required
+2 (left-heavy) Left child +1 or 0 LL Single right rotation
−2 (right-heavy) Right child −1 or 0 RR Single left rotation
+2 (left-heavy) Left child −1 LR Double: left rotation on left child, then right rotation on node
−2 (right-heavy) Right child +1 RL Double: right rotation on right child, then left rotation on node
Reading the Table
Case naming convention: The letters indicate the direction of the path from the imbalanced node to the newly inserted node (or the heavy path after deletion). "LL" means the imbalance is caused by going Left then Left. "LR" means Left then Right, and so on.
Memory Aid
  • Same direction (LL or RR) → single rotation (opposite to the heavy direction)
  • Different direction (LR or RL) → double rotation (straighten first, then rotate)

3. Single Right Rotation — LL Case

When It Applies

The imbalanced node has BF = +2 and its left child has BF = +1 (or 0). The excess height is on the left-left path.

The Rotation Mechanics

Let the imbalanced node be A, its left child be B, and B's right subtree be T₂:

  1. B becomes the new root of this subtree.
  2. A becomes B's right child.
  3. T₂ (B's former right subtree) becomes A's new left child.

Before and After Diagrams

BEFORE (LL imbalance): A (BF = +2) / \ B T₃ / \ T₁ T₂
AFTER (single right rotation): B (new root) / \ T₁ A / \ T₂ T₃

Why BST Order Is Preserved

Before the rotation, inorder traversal gives: T₁ – B – T₂ – A – T₃. After the rotation, inorder traversal gives: T₁ – B – T₂ – A – T₃. The sequence is identical — the rotation only changed parent-child pointers, not the logical ordering of keys.

Worked Example: LL Case

Scenario: Insert keys 30, 20, 10 into an initially empty AVL tree.

Step 1: Insert 30, then 20 30 (BF = +1) / 20 (BF = 0)
Step 2: Insert 10 → imbalance at 30! 30 (BF = +2) ← imbalanced node / 20 (BF = +1) ← left child is left-heavy → LL case / 10

Apply single right rotation around 30:

  • B = 20 becomes new root
  • A = 30 becomes B's right child
  • B's right subtree (null here) becomes A's left child
Step 3: After LL rotation 20 (BF = 0) / \ 10 30 (BF=0) (BF=0) ✓ AVL property restored ✓ Inorder: 10, 20, 30 (correct BST order)

4. Single Left Rotation — RR Case

When It Applies

The imbalanced node has BF = −2 and its right child has BF = −1 (or 0). The excess height is on the right-right path.

The Rotation Mechanics

Let the imbalanced node be A, its right child be B, and B's left subtree be T₂:

  1. B becomes the new root of this subtree.
  2. A becomes B's left child.
  3. T₂ (B's former left subtree) becomes A's new right child.

Before and After Diagrams

BEFORE (RR imbalance): A (BF = −2) / \ T₁ B / \ T₂ T₃
AFTER (single left rotation): B (new root) / \ A T₃ / \ T₁ T₂

Why BST Order Is Preserved

Inorder before: T₁ – A – T₂ – B – T₃. Inorder after: T₁ – A – T₂ – B – T₃. Identical — the rotation is the mirror image of the LL case.

Worked Example: RR Case

Scenario: Insert keys 10, 20, 30 into an initially empty AVL tree.

Step 1: Insert 10, then 20 10 (BF = −1) \ 20 (BF = 0)
Step 2: Insert 30 → imbalance at 10! 10 (BF = −2) ← imbalanced node \ 20 (BF = −1) ← right child is right-heavy → RR case \ 30

Apply single left rotation around 10:

  • B = 20 becomes new root
  • A = 10 becomes B's left child
  • B's left subtree (null here) becomes A's right child
Step 3: After RR rotation 20 (BF = 0) / \ 10 30 (BF=0) (BF=0) ✓ AVL property restored ✓ Inorder: 10, 20, 30 (correct BST order)

5. Double Left-Right Rotation — LR Case

When It Applies

The imbalanced node has BF = +2 and its left child has BF = −1. The excess height is on the left-right path — a "zig-zag" pattern that a single rotation cannot fix.

Why a Single Rotation Fails Here
If we tried a single right rotation on a zig-zag (LR) pattern, the problem would just move to the other side. The double rotation first straightens the zig-zag into a straight line (making it look like an LL case), then applies the single right rotation.

The Rotation Mechanics (Two Steps)

Let the imbalanced node be A, its left child be B, and B's right child be C:

  1. Step 1 — Left rotation on B: Rotate left around B, making C the new left child of A (straightens the zig-zag).
  2. Step 2 — Right rotation on A: Rotate right around A, making C the new root of this subtree.

Step-by-Step Diagrams

INITIAL (LR imbalance): A (BF = +2) / \ B T₄ / \ T₁ C (B's BF = −1) / \ T₂ T₃

Why BST Order Is Preserved

Inorder at every stage: T₁ – B – T₂ – C – T₃ – A – T₄. Neither step changes this ordering — each rotation only reassigns child pointers.

Worked Example: LR Case

Scenario: Insert keys 30, 10, 20 into an initially empty AVL tree.

Step 1: Insert 30, then 10 30 (BF = +1) / 10 (BF = 0)
Step 2: Insert 20 → imbalance at 30! 30 (BF = +2) ← imbalanced node (A) / 10 (BF = −1) ← left child is RIGHT-heavy → LR case! \ 20 (C)

Step 1 — Left rotation around 10:

Intermediate state: 30 (BF = +2) / 20 / 10 Now it looks like an LL case!

Step 2 — Right rotation around 30:

Final result: 20 (BF = 0) / \ 10 30 (BF=0) (BF=0) ✓ AVL property restored ✓ Inorder: 10, 20, 30 (correct BST order)

6. Double Right-Left Rotation — RL Case

When It Applies

The imbalanced node has BF = −2 and its right child has BF = +1. The excess height is on the right-left path — the mirror of the LR case.

The Rotation Mechanics (Two Steps)

Let the imbalanced node be A, its right child be B, and B's left child be C:

  1. Step 1 — Right rotation on B: Rotate right around B, making C the new right child of A (straightens the zig-zag).
  2. Step 2 — Left rotation on A: Rotate left around A, making C the new root of this subtree.

Step-by-Step Diagrams

INITIAL (RL imbalance): A (BF = −2) / \ T₁ B / \ C T₄ (B's BF = +1) / \ T₂ T₃

Why BST Order Is Preserved

Inorder at every stage: T₁ – A – T₂ – C – T₃ – B – T₄. The ordering is maintained throughout both rotation steps.

Worked Example: RL Case

Scenario: Insert keys 10, 30, 20 into an initially empty AVL tree.

Step 1: Insert 10, then 30 10 (BF = −1) \ 30 (BF = 0)
Step 2: Insert 20 → imbalance at 10! 10 (BF = −2) ← imbalanced node (A) \ 30 (BF = +1) ← right child is LEFT-heavy → RL case! / 20 (C)

Step 1 — Right rotation around 30:

Intermediate state: 10 (BF = −2) \ 20 \ 30 Now it looks like an RR case!

Step 2 — Left rotation around 10:

Final result: 20 (BF = 0) / \ 10 30 (BF=0) (BF=0) ✓ AVL property restored ✓ Inorder: 10, 20, 30 (correct BST order)

7. The Subtree Preservation Principle

Core Principle
Rotations only reassign child pointers — they never copy, delete, or reorder keys. Every subtree that existed before the rotation still exists after it, attached at a position that maintains BST inorder ordering.

What Exactly Changes During a Rotation?

In every rotation (single or double), exactly three pointer reassignments occur per single rotation step:

Pointer ChangeLL (Right Rotation) Example
1. Parent of A now points to BB becomes new subtree root
2. A.left ← B.right (T₂)The "orphaned" subtree is adopted
3. B.right ← AA becomes B's child

Why Ordering Is Always Preserved

Consider the general LL case. Before the rotation, BST ordering guarantees:

T₁ < B < T₂ < A < T₃

After the rotation, T₂ moves from being B's right subtree to being A's left subtree. Since B < T₂ < A was already true, placing T₂ as A's left child is valid — everything in T₂ is still less than A and greater than B.

Key Insight for Double Rotations
In LR and RL cases, the intermediate step is also a valid BST (just not balanced). Each individual rotation in the double rotation preserves BST ordering independently, so the composition of two order-preserving operations is also order-preserving.

Subtrees Are Never "Lost"

A common student concern: "What happens to T₂ when B moves up?" The answer: T₂ is explicitly reassigned to a new parent. No subtree is ever orphaned or duplicated. This is why rotations are O(1) operations — they involve a constant number of pointer changes regardless of tree size.

9. Knowledge Checks

These are ungraded self-assessment questions. Use them to verify your understanding before moving to Module 16.3. Select your answer and click "Check" for immediate feedback.

Check 1: Identifying the LR Case

A node X has balance factor +2. Its left child Y has balance factor −1. What rotation type is needed?

✓ Correct! The imbalanced node is left-heavy (BF = +2) but its left child is right-heavy (BF = −1). This zig-zag pattern is the LR case, requiring a double rotation: first left around Y, then right around X.
✗ Not quite. Look at the decision table: BF = +2 at the imbalanced node means it's left-heavy, and BF = −1 at the left child means the heavy side goes right from there. Left then Right = LR case (double left-right rotation). A single rotation won't work because the path "zig-zags."

Check 2: Pointer Reassignment in LL Rotation

In a single right rotation (LL case), node A is the imbalanced node and B is its left child. After the rotation, what becomes A's new left child?

✓ Correct! B's right subtree (T₂) becomes A's new left child. This is the key pointer reassignment — T₂ is "adopted" by A because B no longer needs it on its right (A is now B's right child instead). Since T₂ > B and T₂ < A, placing it as A's left child preserves BST ordering.
✗ Not quite. When B rotates up to become the new root, A becomes B's right child. But A needs a left child — its original left child (B) has moved up. The subtree that fills this gap is B's right subtree (T₂), which is guaranteed to contain values between B and A in BST ordering.

Check 3: BST Property Preservation

Why do AVL rotations always preserve the BST inorder ordering of keys?

✓ Correct! This is the subtree preservation principle. Rotations only change which node is the parent of which — they never move keys between nodes, never copy values, and never reorder subtrees. The inorder sequence is determined by the left-root-right relationship, and rotations carefully maintain this by placing "orphaned" subtrees exactly where BST ordering requires them.
✗ Not quite. Rotations do not sort, swap values, or rebuild anything. They are purely structural operations that reassign child pointers. The key insight is that when a subtree is "displaced" by a rotation, it is placed at the only position where BST ordering allows it. No keys are ever moved between nodes — only the parent-child relationships change.

Check 4: Full Rotation Execution

Consider this AVL tree after inserting 40, 60, 50:

40 (BF = −2) \ 60 (BF = +1) / 50

What is the correct result after the appropriate rotation(s)?

✓ Correct! This is an RL case: node 40 has BF = −2 (right-heavy) and its right child 60 has BF = +1 (left-heavy). Step 1: right rotation around 60 makes 50 the right child of 40 with 60 as 50's right child. Step 2: left rotation around 40 makes 50 the new root with 40 on the left and 60 on the right. Inorder: 40, 50, 60 ✓
✗ Not quite. Identify the case: node 40 is right-heavy (BF = −2) and its right child 60 is left-heavy (BF = +1). Right then Left = RL case. Apply: (1) right rotation around 60 → straightens to 40-50-60 in a line, (2) left rotation around 40 → 50 becomes root. Result: root=50, left=40, right=60. Inorder check: 40 < 50 < 60 ✓
