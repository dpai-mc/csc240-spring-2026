CSC 240 — Discrete Structures  |  Week 9

Discrete Probability

From counting possibilities to measuring likelihood — and putting probability to work in computing.

👋 Welcome & Weekly Focus

Welcome to Week 9! This week we turn the counting and combinatorics skills you built in Weeks 7 and 8 into a powerful new tool: discrete probability. You already know how to count outcomes systematically; now you will assign likelihoods to those outcomes, reason about uncertainty, and use probability to make decisions in computational contexts.

Week 9 sits at an important crossroads in the course. The combinatorial reasoning from the last two weeks flows directly into probability spaces and expected value, and the structured, recursive thinking you will begin developing here carries forward into Week 10, where recursion takes center stage and you will start building the Probability Calculator module for Project 2.

🎯 Week 9 Learning Goals

By the end of this week, you should be able to:

  1. Define a sample space and a probability measure, and calculate basic probabilities for equally likely outcomes.
  2. Apply the complement rule and inclusion-exclusion to find probabilities of unions and intersections of events.
  3. Calculate and interpret conditional probability, and explain what it means for one event to depend on another.
  4. Determine whether two events are independent and use the multiplication rule for independent events.
  5. State and apply Bayes' Theorem to update probabilities given new evidence, including in computing and classification contexts.
  6. Define a discrete random variable, construct its probability distribution, and compute its expected value.
  7. Explain how Monte Carlo simulation uses randomness to estimate probabilities, and describe at least one application in computer science.

🔑 Key Concepts to Watch For

Keep these ideas front of mind as you work through the modules:

  • Sample Space — the complete set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment.
  • Probability Measure — a function that assigns a number between 0 and 1 to each event, satisfying the axioms of probability.
  • Conditional Probability — the probability of an event given that another event has already occurred.
  • Independence — two events are independent when knowing one occurred gives no information about the other.
  • Bayes' Theorem — a formula for reversing conditional probabilities; foundational in spam filters, medical testing, and machine learning classifiers.
  • Expected Value — the long-run average outcome of a random variable, computed as a probability-weighted sum.
  • Monte Carlo Simulation — a computational technique that uses repeated random sampling to estimate probabilities and numerical results.
