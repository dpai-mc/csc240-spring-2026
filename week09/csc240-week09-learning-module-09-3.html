Bayes' Theorem

Week 9 of 17  (March 16-22, 2026) Estimated time: 1.5 hours CSLO 2 · Objectives 3 & 5

1. Opening: The Reverse Question

In Module 9.2 you learned to compute P(A | B) — the probability of event A given that event B has already occurred. The formula is clean and direct:

P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) P(B)

But real-world problems often hand us information in the opposite direction. Consider these scenarios:

  • A medical test returns positive. You know the test's accuracy rate (P(\text{positive} | \text{disease})), but what you actually need is P(\text{disease} | \text{positive}).
  • An email contains the word "free." You know how often spam emails use that word (P(\text{"free"} | \text{spam})), but you need P(\text{spam} | \text{"free"}).
  • A network intrusion detection system flags a packet. You know the false-positive rate, but you need the probability the flag indicates a real attack.
"We know how to find P(A | B). But what if we know P(B | A) and need to find P(A | B)? This is exactly what Bayes' theorem answers."

In each case, we know the probability of observing the evidence given a hypothesis, but we want to flip it: given the evidence, how likely is the hypothesis? Bayes' theorem is the mathematical tool that performs this reversal — and it follows directly from definitions you already know.

🔗 Connection — Module 9.2
Bayes' theorem requires two results from Module 9.2: the conditional probability definition P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B)/P(B) and the law of total probability. If either of those feels uncertain, review Module 9.2 before continuing.

2. Deriving Bayes' Theorem

We derive Bayes' theorem in four labeled steps. Each step is collapsible so you can work through the logic at your own pace. Try to predict the next step before expanding it.

▶ Step 1 Start with the conditional probability definition

By definition (Module 9.2), for any two events A and B with P(B) > 0:

P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) P(B)

Our goal is to express the numerator P(A ∩ B) using P(B | A) — the conditional probability in the opposite direction.

▶ Step 2 Apply the multiplication rule to rewrite the numerator

The multiplication rule (also from Module 9.2) states that for any events A and B:

P(A ∩ B) = P(B | A) · P(A)

This is symmetric: P(A ∩ B) = P(B | A) · P(A) = P(A | B) · P(B).

Substituting into the conditional probability formula from Step 1:

P(A | B) = P(B | A) · P(A) P(B)

This is already a valid form of Bayes' theorem — but the denominator P(B) is often unknown directly. Step 3 addresses that.

▶ Step 3 Expand the denominator using the law of total probability

Suppose B₁, B₂, …, Bₙ form a partition of the sample space — they are mutually exclusive, exhaustive events. The law of total probability (Module 9.2) states:

P(A) = P(A | B1)P(B1) + P(A | B2)P(B2) + ⋯ + P(A | Bn)P(Bn) = ∑i P(A | Bi)P(Bi)

In the two-event case (just B and its complement Bᶜ), this simplifies to:

P(A) = P(A | B)P(B) + P(A | Bc)P(Bc)
▶ Step 4 Combine to get the full form of Bayes' theorem

Substituting the law of total probability into the denominator of the formula from Step 2, and writing it for a general partition B₁, B₂, …, Bₙ, we obtain Bayes' theorem:

P(Bi | A) = P(A | Bi) · P(Bi) ∑j P(A | Bj) · P(Bj)

For the common two-event case (hypothesis H and its complement Hᶜ), this becomes:

P(H | E) = P(E | H) · P(H) P(E | H)P(H) + P(E | Hc)P(Hc)

where H is the hypothesis and E is the observed evidence. This two-event form is what we will use in both worked examples.

⚠️ Nothing New — Just Rearranged
Bayes' theorem is not a new axiom. Every symbol in the formula appeared in Module 9.2. The theorem is simply the result of substituting the multiplication rule and the law of total probability into the conditional probability definition. Its power comes from which direction it lets you compute — from evidence to hypothesis rather than hypothesis to evidence.

3. Vocabulary: Prior, Likelihood, and Posterior

Bayes' theorem has a standard vocabulary that names each component of the formula. Learning these terms is important: they appear in statistics, machine learning, and algorithm analysis literature.

📘 Definition — Bayesian Vocabulary

Given hypothesis H and evidence E:

Prior probability — P(Bᵢ) or P(H)
The probability of the hypothesis before observing any evidence. This reflects background knowledge or population rates (e.g., the prevalence of a disease in the general population). It is called "prior" because it exists prior to the new observation.

Likelihood — P(A | Bᵢ) or P(E | H)
The probability of observing the evidence given that the hypothesis is true. This is what a test or sensor measures (e.g., the probability a test is positive given the patient has the disease). Note: the likelihood is not itself a probability over hypotheses — it is a probability over evidence.

Posterior probability — P(Bᵢ | A) or P(H | E)
The probability of the hypothesis after incorporating the observed evidence. This is what Bayes' theorem computes. The posterior is the prior updated by the likelihood of the evidence. It is called "posterior" because it comes after the observation.

Memory aid: Prior → observe evidence → Posterior. The likelihood is the bridge between them.

Mapping these terms onto the two-event formula makes the structure explicit:

P(H | E) [posterior] = P(E | H) [likelihood] · P(H) [prior] P(E) [total probability of evidence]

4. Worked Example 1 — Medical Diagnostic Testing

This example is a model for PS-06 Problem 5. Work through every step carefully.

▶ Example 1 — Medical Test: What Does a Positive Result Really Mean?

Setup

A disease affects 1% of the population. A diagnostic test has:

  • Sensitivity (true positive rate): 99% — if a patient has the disease, the test is positive 99% of the time.
  • Specificity (true negative rate): 95% — if a patient does not have the disease, the test is negative 95% of the time.

A randomly selected person tests positive. What is the probability they actually have the disease?

Defining the Events

  • D = "patient has the disease"
  • Dᶜ = "patient does not have the disease"
  • T⁺ = "test result is positive"

Known Probabilities

Symbol Meaning Value
P(D) Prior — disease prevalence 0.01
P(Dᶜ) Prior — no disease 0.99
P(T⁺ | D) Likelihood — sensitivity 0.99
P(T⁺ | Dᶜ) Likelihood — false positive rate = 1 − specificity 0.05

Step-by-Step Calculation

  • Identify the partition. D and Dᶜ partition the sample space (every person either has the disease or does not). This satisfies the requirement for the law of total probability.
  • Compute the denominator using the law of total probability. P(T⁺) is the total probability of a positive test across both groups:
    P(T+) = P(T+ | D) · P(D) + P(T+ | Dc) · P(Dc)
    = (0.99)(0.01) + (0.05)(0.99) = 0.0099 + 0.0495 = 0.0594
  • Apply Bayes' theorem.
    P(D | T+) = P(T+ | D) · P(D) P(T+) = 0.0099 0.0594 ≈ 0.1667
  • Interpret the result. Even with a positive test, the probability of actually having the disease is only about 16.7%.

Labeled Probability Table

It often helps to think in terms of a population of 10,000 people to make the numbers concrete:

Has Disease (D) No Disease (Dᶜ) Total
Test Positive (T⁺) 99 (true positives) 495 (false positives) 594
Test Negative (T⁻) 1 (false negative) 9,405 (true negatives) 9,406
Total 100 9,900 10,000

Of the 594 people who test positive, only 99 truly have the disease:

P(D | T+) = 99 594 ≈ 0.167 = 16.7%

Why Is This Surprising?

Most people's intuition says: "The test is 99% accurate — so a positive result must mean I almost certainly have the disease." But the calculation shows the posterior is only ~16.7%. The reason is the low base rate: only 1 in 100 people has the disease. Even a small false-positive rate (5%) applied to the large healthy population (9,900 people) generates far more false positives (495) than the true positives from the small diseased group (99).

The prior probability (prevalence) dominates the result when it is very small. This is called base-rate neglect when people ignore it — and it is addressed in the Watch Out section.

🔗 Why Does This Matter in CS? — Intrusion Detection and Anomaly Detection

The medical testing scenario is structurally identical to network intrusion detection and anomaly detection in computing systems:

  • Replace "disease" with "network intrusion" and "positive test" with "IDS alert." Attack events are rare (low prior), but even a 1% false positive rate on millions of legitimate packets generates enormous numbers of false alarms.
  • In anomaly detection (e.g., fraud detection, malware classification), the base rate of the anomaly is typically very low. A high-accuracy classifier can still produce mostly false positives in production — exactly the phenomenon Bayes' theorem quantifies.
  • Understanding this math is why security engineers tune detection thresholds carefully and why no real system operates with a binary "alert / no alert" decision without considering the base rate.

5. Worked Example 2 — Spam Filtering and Naïve Bayes

▶ Example 2 — Spam Filter: Is This Email Spam?

Conceptual Overview

Email spam filters use a technique called naïve Bayes classification. The core idea is to apply Bayes' theorem to classify an email as spam or not-spam based on the words it contains.

Let S = "email is spam" and W = "email contains the word 'free'." The filter wants to compute:

P(S | W) = P(W | S) · P(S) P(W | S)P(S) + P(W | Sc)P(Sc)

The "naïve" part refers to an independence assumption: when multiple words are considered simultaneously, the filter assumes each word's occurrence is independent of the others given the class. This simplifies the computation enormously and works surprisingly well in practice.

A Simplified Numerical Example

Suppose a spam filter has learned the following from its training data:

SymbolMeaningValue
P(S) Prior — fraction of all email that is spam 0.40
P(Sᶜ) Prior — fraction that is legitimate 0.60
P(W | S) Likelihood — "free" appears in spam emails 0.80
P(W | Sᶜ) Likelihood — "free" appears in legitimate emails 0.10
  • Compute the denominator.
    P(W) = (0.80)(0.40) + (0.10)(0.60) = 0.32 + 0.06 = 0.38
  • Apply Bayes' theorem.
    P(S | W) = (0.80)(0.40) 0.38 = 0.32 0.38 ≈ 0.842
  • Interpret. Given that the email contains the word "free," there is approximately an 84.2% probability it is spam. A filter might mark any email above a threshold (e.g., 0.75) as spam and move it to the junk folder.

In a real spam filter, dozens or hundreds of word features are combined using the naïve independence assumption, and the prior P(S) is continuously updated as the user marks emails as spam or not-spam. The core math, however, is exactly the single-feature Bayes' calculation above — applied repeatedly.

🔗 Connection — This Is the Math Behind Your Email Spam Filter
Naïve Bayes classifiers are among the oldest and most widely deployed machine learning algorithms. Gmail, Outlook, and virtually every major email service use variants of this approach. The formula you just applied is the mathematical core of a system that processes billions of emails per day. When you study machine learning or natural language processing, you will encounter this derivation again — and now you will already know where it comes from.

6. Watch Out — Reasoning Pitfalls

⚠️ Watch Out — Two Classic Errors in Probabilistic Reasoning

Pitfall 1: Base-Rate Neglect

The error: Focusing only on the likelihood P(E | H) and ignoring the prior P(H) when estimating the posterior P(H | E).

Counterexample: In Worked Example 1, the test has 99% sensitivity. A naive reasoner concludes: "99% accurate test, positive result → 99% chance of disease." But the correct posterior is only ~16.7% because the disease is rare (prior = 1%). The low base rate overwhelms the high likelihood. Always identify and use the prior.

In computing: A security alert system that is 99% accurate at detecting attacks will still produce mostly false positives if attacks are rare (say, 1 in 10,000 packets). Ignoring the base rate leads to alert fatigue and wasted analyst time.

Pitfall 2: The Prosecutor's Fallacy

The error: Confusing P(E | H) with P(H | E) — treating the probability of the evidence given innocence as if it were the probability of innocence given the evidence.

Counterexample: A DNA match has a 1-in-a-million false positive rate, so a prosecutor argues: "There is only a 1-in-a-million chance an innocent person matches — therefore the defendant is almost certainly guilty." But this confuses P(\text{match} | \text{innocent}) = 0.000001 with P(\text{innocent} | \text{match}). In a city of 1,000,000 innocent people, roughly 1 innocent person would also match — and that person could be the defendant. The posterior depends on the prior probability of guilt, which the prosecutor has not established.

In computing: Log analysis, forensic auditing, and anomaly detection all involve reasoning from observed evidence to a hypothesis. The prosecutor's fallacy is a constant risk when presenting probabilistic evidence to non-technical stakeholders. Always distinguish P(E | H) from P(H | E) explicitly.

Bayes' Theorem — Reference Card

The Formula (two-event form)

P(H | E) = P(E | H) · P(H) P(E | H)P(H) + P(E | Hc)P(Hc)

General Form (partition B₁, B₂, …, Bₙ)

P(Bi | A) = P(A | Bi) · P(Bi) ∑j P(A | Bj) · P(Bj)
Prior probability — P(Bᵢ) or P(H)
Probability of the hypothesis before observing evidence. Reflects background rates or population prevalence.
Likelihood — P(A | Bᵢ) or P(E | H)
Probability of observing the evidence given the hypothesis is true. This is what a test or sensor measures.
Posterior probability — P(Bᵢ | A) or P(H | E)
Probability of the hypothesis after incorporating the evidence. This is what Bayes' theorem computes.

Two-Step Process

  1. Identify the partition. Determine the mutually exclusive, exhaustive set of hypotheses B₁, B₂, …, Bₙ (often just H and Hᶜ). Write down the prior P(Bᵢ) and likelihood P(A | Bᵢ) for each.
  2. Apply the formula. Compute the denominator using the law of total probability, then divide the numerator P(A | Bᵢ) · P(Bᵢ) by that denominator to obtain the posterior P(Bᵢ | A).

9. Module Summary

📋 Module 9.3 — Key Takeaways
  • Bayes' theorem derives directly from the conditional probability definition and the law of total probability. It answers the "reverse" question: given that we observe evidence E, what is the updated probability of hypothesis H? The three key quantities are the prior P(H), the likelihood P(E | H), and the resulting posterior P(H | E).
  • The base rate dominates when the prior is very small. A high-accuracy test applied to a rare condition can still produce a posterior that is surprisingly low — as the medical testing example (posterior ≈ 16.7% despite 99% sensitivity) demonstrates. This same phenomenon governs intrusion detection, anomaly detection, and spam filtering in computing systems.
  • Two critical reasoning pitfalls — base-rate neglect (ignoring the prior) and the prosecutor's fallacy (confusing P(E | H) with P(H | E)) — are avoided by applying Bayes' theorem explicitly and always identifying the prior before computing the posterior.
