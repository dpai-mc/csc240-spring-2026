Week 15 of 17

Introduction to Trees

CSC 240 – Discrete Structures • Spring 2026

Welcome to Week 15! This week we turn our attention to trees — one of the most important structures in computer science. Trees organize data hierarchically, power search algorithms, represent mathematical expressions, and form the backbone of databases, compilers, and file systems.

Week 15 is the third week of our graph-and-tree unit. You've spent Weeks 12–14 building graph fundamentals, mastering BFS/DFS, and implementing shortest-path and spanning-tree algorithms. Now we specialize: trees are graphs with elegant constraints (connected and acyclic), and that simplicity unlocks powerful operations. Next week (Week 16) you'll extend these ideas to self-balancing AVL trees and hashing.

This is a "learning module week" — your only graded deliverable is PS-10 (20 pts), and there is no Programming Exercise or discussion assignment. That lighter load is intentional: invest the extra time in deeply understanding the module content, interacting with the visualizers, and making progress on your Final Project (especially Component 4: Tree-Based Internal Structure). The concepts you build this week appear directly on Quiz 5 and in the Final Project.

Week 15 Learning Goals

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

  • Define and correctly use fundamental tree vocabulary — root, parent, child, sibling, leaf, internal node, height, depth, level, and subtree.
  • State and prove basic tree properties, such as: a tree with n vertices has exactly n − 1 edges, and a binary tree of height h has at most 2h+1 − 1 nodes.
  • Perform preorder, inorder, postorder, and level-order traversals by hand on any binary tree and record the correct visit sequence.
  • Implement recursive tree traversals and a queue-based level-order traversal in Java or C++.
  • Build and evaluate expression trees from infix, prefix, and postfix expressions, and convert between notations using traversals.
  • Implement core Binary Search Tree (BST) operations — search, insert, and delete (all three cases) — and analyze their time complexity in best, average, and worst cases.
  • Explain why an unbalanced BST degenerates to O(n) performance and articulate the need for balanced trees (setting up AVL trees in Week 16).

Required Activities This Week

Complete the five learning modules in order, then submit PS-10. Continue working on your Final Project throughout the week.

Learn what makes a tree a tree (connected + acyclic), distinguish rooted from free trees, and master the core vocabulary: root, parent, child, sibling, leaf, internal node, depth, height, level, and subtree. You'll also explore the differences between full, complete, and perfect binary trees and prove key node-count properties.

Explore the four standard traversal orders — preorder (Root-Left-Right), inorder (Left-Root-Right), postorder (Left-Right-Root), and level-order (BFS from root). Hand-trace each traversal on example trees, then study their recursive and queue-based implementations. You'll also connect these to the BFS and DFS algorithms you learned in Week 13.

Discover how compilers represent arithmetic expressions as binary trees (operators at internal nodes, operands at leaves). Practice building expression trees from infix and postfix expressions, evaluating them recursively, and converting between infix, prefix, and postfix notation by choosing the right traversal order.

Study the BST property (left subtree keys < node key < right subtree keys) and implement search, insert, and delete operations. Analyze best-case O(log n) vs. worst-case O(n) performance, see how inserting sorted data creates a degenerate "linked-list" tree, and understand why balanced trees (coming in Week 16) solve this problem.

Key Concepts to Watch For

As you work through the modules, pay special attention to these ideas — they appear on PS-10, Quiz 5, and in the Final Project:

  • Tree terminology precision — being able to identify and name every part of a tree (root, leaf, height, depth, etc.) is the foundation for everything else this week.
  • The four traversal orders — preorder, inorder, postorder, and level-order. Know the visit pattern for each and be able to hand-trace them quickly.
  • Expression tree ↔ notation correspondence — inorder traversal gives infix, preorder gives prefix, postorder gives postfix. This is a favorite exam topic.
  • BST operations and the BST property — search, insert, and especially delete (three cases). Understand why inorder traversal of a BST always produces sorted output.
  • Worst-case degeneration — inserting keys in sorted order creates a linear tree with O(n) operations. This motivates balanced trees in Week 16.
  • Connection to your Final Project — Component 4 requires a working tree implementation with traversal. Start planning your design now.
