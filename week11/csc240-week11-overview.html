Welcome and Weekly Focus

Welcome to Week 11 — the pivotal transition point of the semester. This is the bridge week: the first content week of the second half of CSC 240, and the week where the mathematical tools you have been building since Week 1 connect directly to how algorithms are designed, analyzed, and proven correct.

The first ten weeks gave you the foundations — sets, relations, logic, proof techniques, counting, and probability. Starting this week, those foundations drive algorithm analysis. Mathematical induction meets recursive programming. Recurrence relations become your primary language for expressing and measuring algorithmic complexity.

Week 11 combines what were originally two separate weeks of content into one intensive week per the Rev 1 course restructure. You will move through recursive definitions, algorithm tracing, induction-based correctness proofs, the characteristic equation method, and the Master Theorem. By the end of this week, you will be able to derive — not just recite — why merge sort runs in Θ(n log n).

🌉 Why Week 11 Is the Bridge Week

Weeks 1–10 developed the language and logic of discrete mathematics. Weeks 12–17 apply that logic to graph and tree algorithms. Week 11 is the explicit link. The induction structure from Week 6 becomes the formal tool for proving recursive algorithms correct. Recurrence relations become the method for expressing and solving algorithm complexity. Every remaining week in the course builds directly on what you learn here.

  2. Recognize common recurrence patterns — Fibonacci, sequential search, merge sort, binary search — and connect their closed-form solutions to Big-O and Θ complexity classes.

Key Concepts to Focus On

These are the conceptual core of Week 11. Each one appears in both graded assignments and in Quiz 4.

The Recursion ↔ Induction Structural Parallel

A recursive algorithm and an induction proof share an identical two-part structure: the base case in recursion corresponds to the inductive base, and the recursive case corresponds to the inductive step. This is a formal correspondence — not a loose analogy — that allows you to use mathematical induction to prove any recursive algorithm correct. This is the central CSLO 4 theme and the conceptual core of Module 11.3. If you internalize one idea this week, make it this one.

The Characteristic Equation Method

Given a linear homogeneous recurrence such as F(n) = F(n−1) + F(n−2), you find an exact closed-form solution by forming and solving the characteristic polynomial, then applying initial conditions to determine the unknown constants. This is the algebraic core of Module 11.4 and is directly required by PE-08 component (2). Make sure your polynomial algebra is solid before starting Module 11.4.

Fibonacci closed form: Recurrence: F(n) = F(n−1) + F(n−2), F(0) = 0, F(1) = 1 Char. eqn: r² − r − 1 = 0 Roots: r = (1 ± √5) / 2 → φ = (1+√5)/2, ψ = (1−√5)/2 General soln: F(n) = A·φⁿ + B·ψⁿ Apply I.C.: A = 1/√5, B = −1/√5 (Binet's formula)

The Master Theorem

The Master Theorem provides a fast, pattern-based method to determine the complexity of divide-and-conquer algorithms from a recurrence of standard form — without working through the full characteristic equation. You must know all three cases and be able to identify which applies. This is the primary topic of Module 11.5 and is required in PE-08 component (3).

Standard form: T(n) = a·T(n/b) + f(n), a ≥ 1, b > 1 Case 1: f(n) = O(n^(log_b(a) − ε)) → T(n) = Θ(n^(log_b(a))) Case 2: f(n) = Θ(n^(log_b(a))) → T(n) = Θ(n^(log_b(a)) · log n) Case 3: f(n) = Ω(n^(log_b(a) + ε)) → T(n) = Θ(f(n)) Merge sort: T(n) = 2T(n/2) + Θ(n) → a=2, b=2 → Case 2 → Θ(n log n) Binary search: T(n) = T(n/2) + Θ(1) → a=1, b=2 → Case 2 → Θ(log n)

PE-08 Four-Component Structure

PE-08 has four distinct components, each drawing from a specific module: (1) input and term generation from Modules 11.1 and 11.4; (2) characteristic equation solver from Module 11.4; (3) Master Theorem demonstration from Module 11.5; (4) recursive vs. iterative comparison from Module 11.2. Plan your program structure around these four requirements before writing any code. Four Common Recurrence Patterns

These four patterns appear throughout algorithm analysis and should be recognizable by sight by the end of this week. Knowing them will significantly speed up both Quiz 4 and the Final Project analysis document.

Fibonacci: T(n) = T(n−1) + T(n−2) → exponential growth Sequential search: T(n) = T(n−1) + 1, T(1) = 1 → Θ(n) Merge sort: T(n) = 2T(n/2) + n, T(1) = 1 → Θ(n log n) Binary search: T(n) = T(n/2) + 1, T(1) = 1 → Θ(log n)

Connecting to What You Already Know

🔗 Week 6 — Mathematical Induction (Direct Foundation)

In Week 6, you proved summation formulas and divisibility properties using mathematical induction: establish a base case, assume the statement true for k, then show it must hold for k + 1. That exact two-part structure — base case and inductive step — is the structure of every recursive algorithm you encounter this week.

When Module 11.3 asks you to prove that a recursive factorial function correctly computes n!, you are writing a Week 6 induction proof applied to code. Strong induction — also from Week 6 — is the foundation for structural induction on recursively defined sets. If your Week 6 induction proofs felt unsteady, review them before starting Module 11.3.

📋 Week 10 — Review Week (Immediate Prior Week)

Week 10 was an ungraded review week designed to consolidate Weeks 1–9 and bridge into Week 11. If you completed the suggested Week 10 activities, you reviewed induction proofs and worked through early recurrence examples. If not, do so now — your fluency with induction directly determines how quickly Module 11.3 will click.

Prior Knowledge Map

  • Weeks 5–6 — Proof Techniques and Induction: Direct foundation for Module 11.3 — correctness proofs and termination arguments for recursive algorithms.
  • Week 6 — Well-Ordering Principle: Used to prove termination — every recursive call must strictly reduce toward the base case.
  • Weeks 3–4 — Predicate Logic and Quantifiers: Formal language for stating induction hypotheses precisely in written proofs.
  • CSC 221 prerequisite — Recursive Programming: You have already written recursive functions in Java or C++. This week formalizes what you already know how to do — and proves it correct.
  • MTH 80 prerequisite — Intermediate Algebra: Solving quadratic and polynomial equations is essential for the characteristic equation method in Module 11.4. Review polynomial factoring and the quadratic formula before starting Module 11.4 if needed.
